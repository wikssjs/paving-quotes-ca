What affects the price of paving in Mill Courtland Woodside Park

Permits & regulations in Ontario

In Ontario, paving a private driveway within your own property usually does not require a building permit. However, the moment the work affects municipal assets or stormwater function, approvals are often required. In practical terms, Mill Courtland Woodside Park homeowners should expect that these projects typically DO require municipal approval/permits: new or modified curb cuts/driveway entrances in the municipal road allowance, any changes to boulevard areas, and any work that alters storm drainage patterns (for example, modifying catch basins, adjusting existing drainage swales, or connecting/redirecting drainage so runoff no longer matches the existing municipal system). Work that typically does NOT require a permit is resurfacing or rebuilding strictly on private property with no changes to curb/boulevard or municipal drainage.

Step-by-step, here’s how to keep it safe and verify your contractor the Ontario way. First, have them provide their proof of business and trade coverage (a current clearance letter and/or the appropriate registration details from their licensing status). Second, ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing general liability coverage and confirm the policy is active for the project dates. Third, request WSIB/WCB documentation (or exemption letters where applicable) so you’re not left exposed if a worker is injured on-site. Finally, insist on utility locates: call Ontario One Call before any excavation so they can mark buried services before your contractor digs for base and subdrain.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Mill Courtland Woodside Park driveways

For Mill Courtland Woodside Park driveways, asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete all can perform well—if the base is engineered for Toronto’s freeze–thaw movement and drainage is handled properly. Asphalt is usually the lowest upfront option, and when it’s sealed (typically every 3–5 years) it resists moisture better during winter cycling. Interlocking concrete pavers have the highest curb appeal and, importantly, they’re repair-friendly: individual units can be lifted, the base corrected, and the surface reinstalled without tearing up the whole driveway. Poured concrete sits in the middle on upfront cost, but it’s more sensitive to jointing and air-entrainment—if it’s not designed for freeze–thaw, you can see cracking sooner, especially with salt and plough impacts.

In Toronto’s frost environment (about 3–4 feet freezing), all three options need a deeper compacted granular base and good slope control so water can’t sit under the surface. With asphalt, a well-built base and a freeze–thaw-rated mix help prevent early surface break-up. With pavers, proper edge restraints and correct joint sand depth allow minor movement without opening large gaps. With concrete, you need the right mix (including air entrainment), correct control joints, and drainage planning so runoff doesn’t undermine the slab.

Which holds up best to the local routine? Typically, asphalt and pavers do well under routine snow clearing when the base is right—ploughing is easier on pavers because repairs are localized. Concrete can also last a long time, but it demands disciplined crack control to avoid cosmetic spalling or mid-panel cracking. A quick price example: if an asphalt driveway is quoted in the $3,500–$8,000 range and pavers come in closer to $9,000–$20,000, the extra paver cost is justified when you want long-term curb appeal and the ability to fix a damaged section without redoing the entire surface.

How to choose a paving contractor in Mill Courtland Woodside Park

Choosing a paving contractor in Mill Courtland Woodside Park comes down to verifying coverage, comparing apples-to-apples scopes, and making sure the base and drainage are engineered for Toronto’s freeze–thaw cycles. Start with Ontario licensing and insurance: ask for proof of business registration/clearance and confirm they carry active general liability insurance. Then check WSIB/WCB coverage—request the current clearance letter or exemption documentation so you know whether their workers are covered if something happens on-site. If they can’t provide documents quickly, that’s a warning sign.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes. The best quotes break out labour and materials separately (excavation, granular base, asphalt/pavers/concrete, edging/curbs, disposal, and—if needed—subdrainage). Avoid “lump sum” only pricing unless the scope is extremely detailed. Read the scope line-by-line: ask what’s excluded (for example, hydro/vault relocation, topsoil replacement, sod/seed restoration, disposal quantities, and sawcutting depth for concrete). Confirm whether permits (if your project affects curb cuts or municipal right-of-way) are included or excluded.

Warranty matters too. Ask for workmanship warranty length, product/manufacturer warranty details, and whether the warranty is transferable if you sell the home. For payment schedule, don’t accept more than 10–15% upfront; hold back a portion until the final inspection and cleanup are complete. Finally, timeline: require a start date and completion estimate in writing, especially since the paving season is effectively late spring through early fall and crews can be booked on prime dates.
