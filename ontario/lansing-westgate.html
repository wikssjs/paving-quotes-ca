Driveway paving options and costs in Lansing-Westgate

If you’re planning a new driveway or replacing an older surface in Lansing-Westgate, you’ll typically be choosing between asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete. With a population of 16,164 people in 2021 (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census), Lansing-Westgate is busy enough that contractor availability and prime-date scheduling can matter, especially during the short Ontario paving window. In this part of Toronto, most private driveways serve single-family homes, so durability, winter performance, and curb appeal are usually top priorities.

Toronto’s freeze–thaw cycle is the big cost-shaping factor. Frost can penetrate roughly 3–4 feet in the region, which means your driveway needs a deeper, well-compacted granular base and reliable drainage. Without that, water can sit under the pavement, freeze, and heave the surface—leading to cracking, settlement, and early failure. On top of that, the paving season is effectively late spring through early fall. Hot-mix asphalt placement is best when air and pavement temperatures are warm enough, which concentrates demand and can increase pricing for summer start dates.

In areas such as the Westgate neighbourhood (and the surrounding local streets in Lansing-Westgate), we see strong demand for clean curb lines and engineered base work—often right after homeowners finish grading, re-landscaping, or replacing an older driveway apron.

What affects the price of paving in Lansing-Westgate

In Lansing-Westgate and across the Toronto economic region, it’s common to see quotes for the “same” driveway vary by 30–50%. The reason is usually not the asphalt or concrete itself—it’s what’s underneath and around it: excavation depth, granular base design, drainage corrections, and the labour time to remove and rebuild a driveway properly. Even when two contractors quote the same material, one may include stronger frost-protection base and more compaction passes, while another trims scope to hit a faster schedule.

Toronto’s frost depth and freeze–thaw cycles are decisive cost factors. With ground freezing roughly 3–4 feet deep, driveways need a thicker compacted granular base to prevent heaving and cracking. In colder Ontario conditions, that typically means building up the granular layers to around 6–12 inches (depending on soil conditions and design). Costs rise because deeper base work means more excavation, more truckloads of stone, more labour, and more time for proper compaction.

The paving season is also shorter. Hot-mix asphalt placement generally requires air temperatures above about 10°C, so contractors often concentrate work into late spring through early fall. That schedule pressure can push pricing toward the high end of the asphalt band (for example, installed asphalt commonly lands around $3,500–$8,000). Concrete scheduling can be similar, but the mix, jointing plan, and air-entrainment (plus drainage details) can shift cost within the $6,000–$15,000 concrete band.

Concrete examples in Lansing-Westgate: if your existing driveway base is weak, tear-out and rebase can add cost regardless of finish (broom finish vs exposed aggregate). If you have poor drainage or low spots near the garage apron, contractors may need regrading and sub-drain improvements—often the difference between a mid-range and a higher-end quote.

Permits & regulations in Ontario

In Ontario, paving a private driveway within your own property typically does not require a building permit. Most homeowners in Lansing-Westgate can have a new asphalt or concrete surface installed on their driveway without municipal approvals—provided the work stays entirely on private property and doesn’t modify municipal infrastructure.

That said, work in the municipal right-of-way usually does require approval. Examples include creating a new curb cut, widening or changing the boulevard opening, altering curb and gutter elements, or changing how storm drainage ties into municipal systems. If you’re adjusting grading in the area between your property line and the roadway, it’s also wise to confirm whether any lot-grading rules apply (some municipalities restrict how close a hard surface can be to property lines and drainage features).

Before excavation, call Ontario One Call for utility locates so the contractor (and you) know exactly where services run. Then verify the contractor’s credentials: check the Ontario public directory for their licence status, review the certificate of insurance for current general liability coverage (and ask if they’re adding you as an additional insured if applicable), and confirm WSIB coverage where required (or the appropriate coverage status/exemption documentation the contractor uses). Step-by-step, ask for: (1) licence number and proof of insurance, (2) WSIB clearance or status document, and (3) a written scope that spells out disposal, base depth, drainage corrections, and any permit/approval responsibilities.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Lansing-Westgate driveways

For Lansing-Westgate homeowners dealing with Toronto’s freeze–thaw climate, material choice is really a choice about how the driveway tolerates movement and moisture. Asphalt typically has the lowest upfront cost and can be a very good option when the base is engineered correctly. Its performance depends heavily on drainage and an Ontario-suitable mix design; after installation, you should plan on sealing every 3–5 years to protect the surface from moisture intrusion. In the local market, many homeowners find asphalt lands in the $3,500–$8,000 band for typical driveways.

Interlocking concrete pavers cost more upfront, but they handle local winter abuse well because individual units allow for targeted repairs. If a weak spot develops or a utility trench settles, sections can be lifted, corrected, and reset without tearing out the whole driveway. That repairability is a real advantage in neighbourhoods like Westgate where driveways often see re-grading and landscaping changes over time. Pavers also tend to start in the $9,000–$20,000 range, but curb appeal is usually the reason homeowners choose them.

Poured concrete is mid-range compared with pavers, yet it needs careful jointing and air-entrainment to manage freeze–thaw and de-icing salt. Concrete can crack if drainage is poor or if the base is not compacted to the right thickness for frost protection. For local winters, that’s why you don’t want “thin-lift” shortcuts on base depth.

A practical dollar example: if your driveway is around the high end of the asphalt band—say you’re near $8,000 for a properly based asphalt with quality drainage—choosing pavers at the low-to-mid end (often closer to the beginning of $9,000–$20,000) can make sense if you value long-term appearance and easier repairs. If you simply need a durable, cost-effective surface and are comfortable with sealing, asphalt is usually the best value.

How to choose a paving contractor in Lansing-Westgate

Choosing a contractor in Lansing-Westgate is mostly about verifying credentials and making sure your quote includes the right scope for Toronto’s freeze–thaw conditions. In Ontario, confirm the contractor’s Ontario licence status (ask for the licence number and verify it in the applicable online directory). Next, request a certificate of insurance showing current general liability coverage—review the effective dates and ask about coverage limits. For workers on site, confirm WSIB/WCB coverage: ask for the WSIB clearance letter or the contractor’s current WSIB status documentation (the document should match the company name and be current). If any of these are missing, expect delays or shortcuts.

Get 2–3 itemised written quotes, not lump sums. The best quotes break down excavation, base material type, compaction, drainage/grading work, disposal, and the surface system. Make sure it’s clear what’s excluded: for example, landscaping restoration, replacing catch basins, additional saw-cutting, or removing existing retaining edges. Also confirm whether permits/approvals are included if any right-of-way work is required. For longevity, insist on workmanship warranty terms—how long they cover, what qualifies as a defect, and whether the warranty is transferable to a new owner.

Payment schedule matters. Avoid paying all the money upfront—keep deposits around 10–15% and hold back a portion until completion and cleanup. Ask for the start date and estimated duration in writing so you can plan around contractor scheduling during peak spring and early summer.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Lansing-Westgate

Can asphalt be laid in cold weather in Lansing-Westgate?

In Ontario, asphalt can only be placed when temperatures and conditions support proper compaction and adhesion—so “cold weather asphalt” isn’t really a reliable DIY or off-season option for Lansing-Westgate. Hot-mix asphalt placement typically requires air temperatures above about 10°C (and suitable pavement/aggregate conditions) so the mix can be laid and compacted to density. Toronto-area freeze–thaw cycles mean that if asphalt is placed too early in the season, weak compaction and poor bonding can lead to early cracking and raveling as water enters through micro-features. The best contractor will schedule work in late spring through early fall and will confirm the weather window in writing. If you need an earlier start, ask whether they use approved cold-weather protocols and what they do to protect the surface until it can cure properly.

What is a driveway apron and do I need one in Lansing-Westgate?

A driveway apron is the transitional area that connects your private driveway surface to the curb and/or sidewalk/boulevard zone—essentially the “front” section that vehicles and plows use most often. In Lansing-Westgate, whether you need an apron depends on your existing curb cut, your drainage setup, and whether your driveway currently ties into the street properly. If the apron area is failing, settling, or creating drainage issues, it can increase the risk of heaving and cracking in the first few winters. If any work involves the municipal right-of-way—such as changing the curb cut or modifying boulevard drainage—you’ll need municipal approval rather than assuming it’s part of a standard private driveway paving job. A good quote will clearly state whether the apron replacement is included and whether approvals/permits are part of the scope.

How do I maintain my driveway through Lansing-Westgate winters?

To protect your driveway in an Ontario winter, focus on moisture control and surface integrity. For asphalt in Lansing-Westgate, seal the surface according to the recommended schedule (commonly every 3–5 years depending on sun exposure and traffic) and keep up with crack filling so water can’t migrate into the base. During snow/ice season, avoid aggressive scraping that damages the edges and restraints; pavers and concrete edges are where salt and water can start deterioration. If de-icing is needed, use it carefully—too much salt can increase surface scaling on concrete over time. Also watch for low spots where water pools after thaw; those spots can undermine base stability and accelerate cracking. A contractor who built the right base depth for Toronto’s frost conditions will usually give you better winter performance, but your routine removal methods still matter.

Is it worth sealing a new driveway right away in Lansing-Westgate?

Usually, it’s not “right away,” and the timing matters. For asphalt driveways in Lansing-Westgate, sealing is typically done after the asphalt has cured so the surface can accept the sealer properly. Sealing too early can trap moisture and reduce bond. Most homeowners are advised to wait until the asphalt has cooled and cured and the surface is ready per the contractor’s schedule—often after the initial curing period and once weather conditions are suitable during Ontario’s season. When done at the right time, sealing helps slow oxidation and moisture penetration, which matters a lot in a region with freeze–thaw cycles. If you’re comparing budget options, note that sealing is usually a relatively small line item compared to full replacement. Keeping up with sealing can extend the life of a driveway that would otherwise slide toward earlier patching, which is a real cost saver versus moving into higher repair or replacement bands.

What is a chip seal driveway and is it suited to Lansing-Westgate?

Chip seal (also called tar-and-chip) is a binder-and-stone surface system designed to provide traction and a durable wearing layer. In Lansing-Westgate, chip seal can be suitable for some driveways—particularly where homeowners want a lower upfront cost and are comfortable with periodic maintenance. However, Toronto’s freeze–thaw cycles can expose weak base areas and allow moisture movement if the underlying granular layers aren’t properly engineered. That’s why chip seal isn’t always the best long-term choice if you have known drainage issues, a soft subgrade, or a driveway that sees heavy turning/loading. It also typically needs more frequent maintenance than asphalt or pavers. If you’re budgeting, chip seal often falls lower than standard asphalt (though the exact scope varies), but for many households the better value is engineered asphalt in the $3,500–$8,000 range or pavers for curb appeal and repairability.

How much does driveway crack repair cost in Lansing-Westgate?

Crack repair costs vary based on crack width, the length of repair, surface prep, and whether the repair is just crack filling or includes localized patching. In practice across Lansing-Westgate, small asphalt crack filling is often priced as a minor repair, while wider cracks, edge failures, or areas with pumping water under the surface can trigger a more involved “spot base fix” before sealing. As a planning reference, sealing and repair work commonly sits in the $300–$1,500 band for typical driveway sections, but major repairs can run higher if the base must be corrected. The key is diagnosing why the crack formed—Toronto’s freeze–thaw cycles often reveal drainage problems or inadequate base compaction. A reputable contractor will assess drainage and base conditions before recommending crack-only fixes.
