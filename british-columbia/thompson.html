What affects the price of paving in Thompson

In Thompson, you’ll often see quotes for the same “basic driveway” vary by 30–50% across Lower Mainland–Southwest and across British Columbia because the expensive part usually isn’t the surface—it’s what happens underneath. Two contractors can both offer asphalt, but one may include proper excavation, geotextile separation, and full compacted granular base, while another relies on an average thickness on saturated subgrade. That gap is where cost swings happen.

Frost line depth and freeze–thaw cycles are decisive cost factors in Thompson. While coastal BC generally has milder conditions than the Interior, regional practice still anticipates a frost line on the order of 18 inches for sensitive infrastructure. When base thickness is too thin, water movement in clay-heavy soils can lead to heaving and later cracking. As a rule of thumb from residential construction practice, asphalt driveways often use about 6 inches of compacted granular base plus roughly 2.5–3 inches of asphalt; if your site needs deeper excavation in weak soils, you’re not just paying for asphalt—you’re paying for more base, more compaction, and more haul-away/disposal. Concrete can also rise in cost when thicker sections and reinforcement details are required for your drainage and support conditions.

Climate and scheduling also matter. Even when the paving season is longer than in Northern BC, heavy winter rains and shoulder-season storms compress workable windows, which can tighten availability and scheduling pressure. Asphalt mix design should be freeze–thaw rated; that can shift pricing toward the upper part of the $12–$20 per sq ft band. If you compare options, an asphalt driveway at about $12,50 – $18,00 per sq ft can become more expensive than pavers in a complicated tear-out, drainage, and reconstruction scenario. For a simple, well-draining site, pavers may justify the higher $20,00 – $40,00 band because repair is local rather than whole-surface replacement.

In short: older driveways with buried soft spots, poor slopes, or saturated edges often push total cost upward by requiring added base depth and regrading, even if the surface area doesn’t change.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property usually does not require a building permit because you’re improving an existing lot surface, not changing structural elements of a dwelling. However, work in the municipal right-of-way typically requires approval. That includes new curb cuts, changes to boulevard areas, alterations to roadside drainage, or any adjustments that affect stormwater flow where the municipality has authority. Some municipalities also have lot-grading bylaws that limit how close a hard surface can be placed to property lines and can restrict drainage patterns that send water onto neighbouring properties.

For Thompson homeowners, the practical process is straightforward:

  • Before excavation, arrange utility locates through BC 1 Call to identify buried services.
  • Confirm whether your driveway ties into any boulevard work (curb cut, sidewalk removal, or drainage modifications). If it does, ask the municipality which approvals are required.
  • If your contractor plans regrading near property lines, verify the lot-grading rules with the local municipality so you don’t end up with a rejected inspection or required rework.

Also verify your contractor properly. Ask for their Certificate of Insurance (liability coverage) and confirm they can provide WSIB coverage where applicable for worker protection under provincial requirements. For licensing, check the appropriate contractor registry online for their credentials and confirm the business name matches the quote. Finally, request a clearance letter if the contractor can supply one, and keep copies with your contract so you have documentation if questions come up later.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Thompson driveways

Choosing the right driveway surface in Thompson comes down to how you manage winter moisture and how much maintenance you’re willing to do. Asphalt is typically the lowest upfront option and performs well when the base is properly compacted and drainage is right; the trade-off is that asphalt needs sealing regularly—often every 3–5 years—to slow water intrusion and reduce freeze–thaw cracking. Interlocking concrete pavers cost more upfront, but they’re repairable section-by-section without tearing out the whole driveway, which can be valuable when you have utility access or localized base settlement. Poured concrete sits in the mid-range cost category, but it’s more sensitive to cracking if it’s not properly air-entrained and detailed to handle freeze–thaw movement and de-icing salt.

In the Lower Mainland–Southwest style of construction, we still treat drainage as a primary “freeze–thaw control.” That usually means building around typical residential practice—about 6 inches of compacted granular base under asphalt, and appropriate granular base under pavers—with attention to slope so water moves away from the structure. With plowing and sand/grit in winter, pavers usually take impacts better at the surface level because individual units can be replaced. Asphalt can show rutting or bleeding if the mix and base aren’t designed correctly, especially in saturated clay-heavy soils.

Where does the price difference make sense? If your asphalt quote is around $12,50 – $18,00 per sq ft, a paver option at $20,00 – $40,00 per sq ft can be justified when you want premium curb appeal and the ability to repair only affected panels after a future drainage or utility issue. If your driveway is straightforward—good drainage, stable subgrade—concrete at $18,00 – $35,00 per sq ft can also be a strong value, provided the mix is air-entrained and the base is built to the right depth.

How to choose a paving contractor in Thompson

For paving in Thompson and across British Columbia, start by confirming the contractor is properly set up to work safely and legally. Ask for their Certificate of Insurance and make sure the business name on the COI matches the contractor you’ll sign with. For worker protection coverage, confirm their WSIB/WCB status and request documentation; reputable crews won’t hesitate to provide proof. Licensing varies by trade and job scope, so don’t rely on word-of-mouth—check their credentials through the appropriate online registry and keep a screenshot or printed verification with your quote package.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes, not a single lump-sum number. Itemise labour and materials (excavation, granular base, geotextile separation if included, asphalt thickness, paver base/bedding, edge restraints, disposal, and compaction). Pay special attention to exclusions: is tear-out and hauling included? Are utility locates included or the homeowner’s responsibility? Is the price including curb/boulevard work if your driveway meets the street, and does it include any required approvals? A clear scope prevents “surprise” change orders after excavation begins.

For warranty, insist on workmanship warranty length and what it covers (settlement, raveling, cracking) and whether the warranty is transferable if you sell. Keep the payment schedule conservative: never pay more than about 10–15% upfront, and use a holdback until the job is complete and cleaned. Finally, get the start date and completion estimate in writing so you can plan around winter conditions.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Thompson

How much does it cost to pave a driveway in Thompson?

In Thompson, most homeowners land somewhere within the regional residential pricing bands once you account for excavation, base thickness, and drainage prep. Asphalt driveway paving typically runs about $12,00 – $20,00 per sq ft depending on whether it’s a full new build or an overlay, and on soil conditions. Interlocking pavers usually come in around $20,00 – $40,00 per sq ft because of base depth, bedding sand, and the restraint system. Concrete is commonly in the $18,00 – $35,00 per sq ft range, but air-entrainment and reinforcement choices affect the final number. The biggest cost swing is often base excavation depth and drainage corrections—especially where clay subgrade holds water.

Asphalt vs interlocking pavers vs concrete — which is best in Thompson?

There isn’t one “best” option for everyone in Thompson, but climate-driven performance and maintenance tolerance usually decide it. Asphalt is often the best value if you’re willing to seal every 3–5 years and you get a properly compacted granular base with good slope/drainage. Interlocking concrete pavers are the top choice when you want curb appeal and easy local repairs—if a section settles, it can be lifted and reset without tearing out the entire driveway. Poured concrete can be a great mid-range option, but it must be air-entrained and built on the right base to manage freeze–thaw movement. If you’re using de-icing salt or plowing heavily, pavers and sealed asphalt often tolerate routine winter wear better at the surface level.

How long does a paved driveway last in Thompson?

With typical residential construction and routine winter maintenance, asphalt driveways commonly last 15–20 years when the base is built correctly and the surface is sealed on schedule. Pavers often reach 25–35 years because the system is modular and repairs are localized. Poured concrete usually sits around 20–30 years when it’s air-entrained and cured properly, with good drainage preventing water from pooling at the slab edges. Lifespan is shortened most often by water issues—poor grading, clogged drainage, or subgrade that stays saturated. If your quote includes drainage correction and base depth appropriate to the frost-line expectation used in BC practice, you’ll typically see better long-term results.

When is the best time of year to pave in Thompson?

For Thompson and much of British Columbia, the best paving window is when temperatures are consistently warm enough for asphalt placement and compaction, and when the subgrade isn’t excessively wet. Asphalt work generally needs air temperatures above about 10°C, plus dry conditions so the base can be compacted to specification. That means late spring through summer is usually ideal, with early fall sometimes workable if the weather stabilises. Contractors can still plan in advance to avoid shoulder-season rain delays. If you want the most reliable schedule and quality, book your driveway paving when ground conditions are dry and crews can complete base, leveling, and paving without extended downtime.

How deep should the base be for Thompson's frost?

Base depth in Thompson depends on soil condition and drainage, not just the frost line number. In BC practice, design accounts for a frost depth expectation on the order of 18 inches for sensitive infrastructure, and that’s why contractors focus on proper granular base thickness and water control. For many residential asphalt driveways, the common build-up is about 6 inches of compacted granular base plus 2.5–3 inches of asphalt. In weaker or saturated soils, excavation is usually deeper to reach stable material and to rebuild the required base. For pavers, the base often targets around 6 inches plus bedding sand and pavers. The right answer comes from assessing subgrade and moisture—so your quote should include a base thickness plan, not just a surface thickness.

How often should I seal my asphalt driveway?

Seal your asphalt driveway about every 3–5 years, starting after the surface has cured (often after the initial installation period recommended by the paving contractor). Sealing matters in Thompson because winter moisture can push water into small cracks, and water movement drives freeze–thaw deterioration. If you live in an area with heavier rain exposure or your driveway slopes toward landscaping that stays wet, you may need the shorter end of the interval. If you’re maintaining cracks with timely patching and you keep drainage working (no clogged edges, proper slope), you can often stretch toward the longer interval. Compared with replacing asphalt, sealing is one of the most cost-effective ways to protect your investment. Prices for sealing/repair commonly fall in the $2,00 – $6,00 per sq ft range, depending on scope.
