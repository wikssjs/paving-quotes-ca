In practice, most private driveways are tied to single-detached homes, so the market is very residential—meaning crews are often scheduling multiple smaller jobs rather than one large commercial site, which affects availability during the short paving season.

Around 100 Mile House, areas like the Centennial Drive / Alder Park corridor tend to be in higher demand as homeowners refresh aging driveways ahead of winter.

What affects the price of paving in 100 Mile House

In 100 Mile House and across the Cariboo, even “the same size driveway” can come in 30–50% apart once you compare base build-up, drainage corrections, and how much tear-out is required. Compared with milder parts of British Columbia, contractors also face a tighter work window: hot-mix asphalt typically needs air temperatures above 10°C for reliable placement and compaction, so crews concentrate production into fewer workable months and carry more scheduling pressure. That tends to raise labour and mobilization costs when you’re calling for a last-minute start.

The biggest decisive factor is frost. Freeze–thaw cycles are the reason the granular structure must be built thick and clean. In colder inland regions, the base is often in the 12–18 inch range under driveways to reduce heaving, rutting, and cracking—whereas you may see less build-up in coastal BC. In practical terms, deeper frost requires thicker, well-graded aggregate and careful compaction, and that increases both material and equipment time.

Concrete pricing can also shift based on air-entrainment, reinforcement decisions, and joint layout—choices that improve freeze-thaw durability but add cost. For example, a typical 400 sq ft asphalt replacement can land in the broader $12–$20 per sq ft band if you need full base reconstruction, but it will be closer to the low end if you’re doing a controlled overlay with solid existing drainage. On the concrete side, choosing pavers (often $20–$35 per sq ft) can justify the difference when you need curbside longevity and repairability; meanwhile, poured concrete ($18–$30 per sq ft) can be a good value when the base and drainage are already correct and you’re not dealing with repeated water pooling.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property usually does not require a building permit. Typically, you do not need a permit if the work is limited to resurfacing, replacing the driveway surface, or installing asphalt/concrete/pavers on the property portion of your driveway footprint, as long as you’re not altering municipal infrastructure. However, you do need municipal approval when the work affects the road/servicing area—especially if you’re changing curb cuts, modifying the boulevard, or altering storm drainage patterns (for example, changing the way runoff leaves your site and enters the ditch system).

Here’s a homeowner-friendly step-by-step way to verify a paving contractor in 100 Mile House, British Columbia. First, request their Certificate of Insurance and confirm liability coverage is active for your project dates. Second, verify they carry the proper provincial worker coverage documentation (commonly shown through clearance letters or equivalent proof) and ask what coverage they maintain for their crew. Third, confirm their business licensing status with the appropriate provincial licensing/registration records and ensure their contact info and business address match the paperwork you receive. Finally, before excavation, require utility locates: call BC 1 Call (or the relevant provincial service) so all buried services are marked before any digging or base work begins.

If your driveway ties into ditch flow or you’re changing how water routes around your foundation, ask the contractor to show you the proposed drainage plan in writing.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for 100 Mile House driveways

For 100 Mile House driveways, the choice between asphalt, interlocking concrete pavers, and concrete comes down to upfront cost versus long-term freeze-thaw resilience and how you want the surface to be repaired after winter. Asphalt is usually the lowest upfront option, and it can perform well when built on a thick, well-graded base with solid drainage. The trade-off is maintenance: asphalt typically needs sealing every 3–5 years to protect from oxidation and water intrusion that accelerates winter cracking.

Interlocking pavers cost more up front (often in the $20–$35 per sq ft range), but they’re very forgiving in Cariboo conditions because individual units can be reset if any area shifts. That matters when freeze–thaw cycles move moisture in the base; pavers can be re-leveled without tearing out the entire driveway. Poured concrete sits in the middle ($18–$30 per sq ft) but is more sensitive to shrinkage, improper jointing, and non-air-entrained mix. In British Columbia’s freeze–thaw climate, a properly air-entrained concrete mix and thoughtful control joints help reduce cracking.

Base preparation is the common denominator: in the Cariboo, you should plan for a thicker granular structure to help keep the pavement system stable below frost influence. Drainage design—ditching, grading, and edge drains where needed—often determines whether any surface lasts. If your property sees winter sand, ploughing abrasion, or occasional de-icing use, pavers often hold up best for patch-and-repair scenarios.

To illustrate value, consider a 400 sq ft driveway: asphalt might fall around $12–$20 per sq ft (roughly $4,800–$8,000 before any major surprises), while pavers could be closer to $20–$35 per sq ft (roughly $8,000–$14,000). If you anticipate changing utilities or you want a surface that can be fixed section-by-section after a problematic freeze line, that extra investment can be justified.

How to choose a paving contractor in 100 Mile House

Start by confirming your paving contractor in British Columbia is properly set up to do the work. Ask for proof of liability insurance and check that the certificate lists your project address and is current (not expired). For worker protection, request documentation that shows the contractor’s provincial worker coverage status (often provided as a clearance letter or equivalent). Then verify their business registration and licensing/registration standing—use the contractor’s business name exactly as shown on invoices, and match it to the documentation they provide.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes. You want a breakdown that separates labour and materials (asphalt tonnage or concrete quantities, base gravel/aggregate, geotextile if used, saw-cutting and disposal, and drainage/grading). Avoid quotes that only list a single “lump sum” without stating what’s included. Carefully read exclusions: is tear-out included? Is trucking and disposal included? Will they replace soft spots in the subgrade or is that “extra if needed”? Also clarify whether permits/approvals are required and, if so, who handles them—especially if you’re changing how runoff reaches the ditch or boulevard.

Warranty matters in freeze–thaw climates. Ask for the workmanship warranty length and whether it covers cracking from settlement, edge failures, or base-related movement. Confirm if there’s a manufacturer warranty on materials and whether it’s transferable. For payment, keep it practical: never pay more than 10–15% upfront, and use a holdback until the job is fully complete and cleaned up. Get the start date, expected completion window, and weather-related contingencies in writing, since the Cariboo season can shut down mid-project after thaw.

Frequently asked questions — paving in 100 Mile House

How deep should the base be for 100 Mile House's frost?

In 100 Mile House and the broader Cariboo, base depth is driven by freeze–thaw penetration and moisture susceptibility. For driveways, a common target is roughly 12–18 inches of properly compacted, well-graded granular base beneath asphalt or concrete surfaces, with the exact thickness adjusted to your subgrade conditions and drainage. If the soil holds water or you have observed ponding after rain or snowmelt, you may need additional excavation and base replacement rather than just adding depth. The key is that the base must stay as dry as possible so water doesn’t expand when it freezes. A good contractor should explain their planned depth, material gradation, and drainage approach before quoting—because that’s often what separates a $12 per sq ft type outcome from higher pricing when full rebuild is needed.

How often should I seal my asphalt driveway?

For asphalt in British Columbia’s freeze–thaw environment, sealing is usually a recurring maintenance step rather than a one-time job. Most driveways should be sealed after the asphalt has cured (commonly after 6–12 months, depending on product and weather), and then resealed every 3–5 years. Sealing helps slow water infiltration and oxidation, which matters in the Cariboo where winter freeze cycles can encourage water movement through micro-cracks. If you’re budgeting, it’s smart to treat sealing as part of the long-term cost story: asphalt upfront may land within the $12–$20 per sq ft range, but skipping sealing often shortens service life and increases the chance you’ll be doing an overlay sooner.

Why does my driveway crack and heave in winter?

Cracking and heaving are usually symptoms of a pavement system that’s losing stability under winter moisture and freezing conditions. The most common causes in 100 Mile House are inadequate drainage, a base that’s too thin for frost protection, or saturated frost-susceptible soils under the driveway. When water is trapped in the base or subgrade, it expands during freezing; repeated freeze–thaw cycles then amplify movement, leading to edge breakup, surface cracking, and sometimes pothole-like failures. Another contributor can be poor compaction or grading that doesn’t shed water away from the driveway. If your driveway was paved over old, soft gravel or without addressing drainage, the surface often fails early even if the asphalt looks “fine” in summer.

Do I need a permit to pave my driveway in British Columbia?

In most cases in British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property footprint does not require a building permit. Typically, work such as replacing asphalt, adding pavers, or resurfacing your driveway is considered private property maintenance. However, you should expect municipal approval when the job affects the municipal right-of-way—such as new curb cuts, changes to the boulevard, or modifications that alter storm drainage flow. In 100 Mile House, if your driveway ties into the ditch system or you’re changing how runoff leaves the property, confirm the requirements with the municipality before work starts. Regardless of permits, you should always arrange utility locates before excavation so contractors can dig safely without striking services.

How long before I can drive on new asphalt?

After asphalt paving in the Cariboo, the “driveable” timeline depends on mix design, thickness, temperature, and weather during placement. In general, contractors aim for a cure period where the surface cools and sets enough to prevent tracking or damage. Many residential driveway projects are available to light traffic the same day or shortly after, but heavier vehicle use often requires longer. If temperatures drop quickly or it’s a cool spring/fall day, drying and stabilization can take longer, which is why scheduling within the short paving season matters. Ask your contractor to specify your expected drive-on time in writing, and confirm whether they recommend waiting a set number of hours or until the surface is fully cooled and not tacky. This is especially important when you’re budgeting around a $12–$20 per sq ft type asphalt project—quality placement and cure are part of what you’re paying for.

What causes potholes and how are they repaired?

Potholes typically form when water enters cracks or voids, then freezes and expands, or when traffic forces break down the base. In 100 Mile House’s freeze–thaw climate, a small failure can rapidly turn into a larger crater once moisture is trapped and temperatures swing. Common causes include inadequate base thickness, poor drainage, and edges that lose restraint so the pavement starts to ravel. Repairs usually start with removing loose material down to sound base, then cleaning and correcting drainage or subgrade issues before placing new asphalt in lifts. A proper patch should be compacted to match the existing thickness and sealed if the surrounding area is older. If you see repeated potholes, that’s often a sign the base needs attention—not just surface patching.
