Driveway paving options and costs in Kelowna

Kelowna homeowners typically have two realities to plan around: first, most driveways are tied to detached homes (single-detached houses account for 42.5% of dwellings), and second, the Thompson–Okanagan freeze–thaw cycle puts a lot of stress on the base. According to the 2021 Census, Kelowna has 144,576 residents and a strong base of homeowner households (40,250; 64.7% of households own), which is why paving contractors are busiest during the short, drier working season.

In this interior climate, pricing isn’t just about “paving the top.” Driveways must be built with substantially more granular base and proper drainage so water can’t sit under the surface and expand during cold snaps. That’s a big reason you’ll see contractor availability tighten in spring and early summer: hot-mix asphalt production and placement depend on warm conditions (air temperatures generally need to be above about 10°C), and crews price in weather risk and mobilization.

In Kelowna, demand is especially strong around central neighbourhoods and established areas such as Rutland and Upper Mission, where many older homes (31.7% built before 1981) have driveways that are either undersized for today’s traffic loads or struggling with drainage. If your soil is clayey, your slope is steep, or water drains toward the garage, the job can require deeper excavation and upgraded base—which changes the total cost more than people expect.

What affects the price of paving in Kelowna

In the Thompson–Okanagan and across British Columbia, you can see driveway quote swings of 30–50% even for “the same size” because the real variables are underground: excavation depth, granular base, drainage corrections, and how freeze–thaw-resistant the build-up is. Coastal BC pricing can be lower simply because the freeze–thaw intensity is different and winter shutdowns behave differently. In the interior, the freeze–thaw cycle is decisive, and contractors must design the granular structure to resist heaving, rutting and cracking rather than just placing a surface.

The frost line for buildings is often discussed around 18 inches in much of the interior, but driveways and slabs experience higher moisture and traffic loading. That typically means thicker compacted granular base (often around the 6–12 inch “granular requirement” range in colder regions) and careful sub-grade preparation. In Kelowna, deeper excavation or upgraded granular material is common when soils are clay-heavy, when slopes direct water toward the garage, or when the driveway has settled.

Seasonality matters too. Hot-mix asphalt generally needs air temperatures above about 10°C to place and compact correctly, so crews concentrate work into the drier months and add mobilization and weather risk to pricing. This is one reason a properly built asphalt project can land in the $4,500–$9,000 band, while a paver or higher-finish concrete scope moves into the higher ranges like $6,500–$14,000 or $7,000–$15,000 depending on depth, base, and finish.

Concrete examples: a broom-finished concrete driveway can be a straightforward pour, but exposed aggregate increases material and labour, often pushing total cost toward the upper end of the concrete range. Likewise, if your sub-grade is weak and needs stabilization, concrete can get more expensive quickly because both excavation and base rebuild increase.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property usually does not require a building permit. However, the moment you touch municipal infrastructure, you typically need municipal approval—especially in Kelowna where boulevard work and storm drainage are closely managed. Concrete or asphalt work that commonly does require approval includes new curb cuts, changing an existing boulevard opening, reworking the storm drain tie-in, or altering grading in the right-of-way. Work that often does not require a permit is standard replacement on private property footprint (for example, tearing out and repaving inside your legal lot line), as long as you’re not changing municipal drainage patterns or access points.

Before excavation, always get utility locates using the appropriate provincial call system (BC 1 Call). That’s not optional—hitting underground services can be dangerous and can also delay the paving schedule.

To verify a Kelowna contractor is legitimately covered in BC, follow a simple checklist. First, confirm their licence/registration status through the appropriate provincial registry for their contracting category. Next, request a certificate of insurance showing general liability coverage for the amount stated on the quote. Ask for WSIB/WCB coverage documentation if the contractor is using workers and operating as a business with employees. Finally, insist on an up-to-date clearance letter where applicable and keep copies for your records. A reputable contractor will provide these documents quickly and will match the insured name to the person signing the contract.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Kelowna driveways

For Kelowna’s freeze–thaw climate and winter maintenance routine, your choice of driveway material comes down to how it handles water movement and temperature cycling. Asphalt usually has the lowest upfront cost and provides a smooth surface that’s easy for plows and sand to work with, but it needs sealing every 3–5 years to slow aging and reduce water penetration. Interlocking concrete pavers cost more initially, yet they shine in Kelowna when you need repairability: individual stones can be lifted and reset if settlement or a utility issue ever occurs. Poured concrete sits in the middle on typical budgeting, but freeze–thaw performance depends heavily on air entrainment, proper mix, and jointing; de-icing can also increase surface wear if the concrete isn’t specified for durability.

Across the Thompson–Okanagan, all three options still require a well-designed granular base and drainage. In practice, we build a thicker base for driveways than a “minimum” frost assumption because moisture management is critical—especially where downspouts discharge toward the garage or where the driveway crosses clay soils. A good slope to the road, properly constructed edges, and clean-out of any swales help prevent the water that turns to ice and expands under load.

Where the price difference is justified: if your scope is pushing into the $6,500–$14,000 paver band, it’s often because you’re gaining curb appeal and easier spot repairs. If you’re trying to maximize durability per dollar and your driveway doesn’t have complicated transitions, asphalt in the $4,500–$9,000 band can be a strong fit—provided it’s built on a proper base and sealed on schedule.

How to choose a paving contractor in Kelowna

Choosing a contractor in Kelowna is mostly about verification and clarity. First, make sure the company can legally perform the work in British Columbia and that they provide current general liability insurance. If they use workers (even a small crew), ask for WSIB/WCB coverage documentation and confirm the certificate aligns with the legal business name. To check, request the certificate of insurance and any clearance letter directly and verify the documents are not expired; most reputable contractors will send them the same day. You should also verify their registration/licence status through the relevant provincial online registry before signing anything.

Second, get 2–3 itemised written quotes—not just a single line “turnkey” price. The best quotes break down labour and material, including excavation, base preparation, asphalt/concrete/paver supply, compaction testing (if included), forms/edges, and any required permits or right-of-way approvals. Scope exclusions matter: confirm what’s included for tear-out, grading, drainage corrections, and disposal of old asphalt or concrete. If you’re paying for sealing (asphalt), make sure the quote states the timing—typically after the asphalt cures.

Third, review warranty and payment terms. Workmanship warranties should be written and specific about what’s covered (for example, premature failure or settlement). Product warranties depend on the manufacturer and may not cover installation issues. Payment schedule should protect you: never pay more than 10–15% upfront; hold back a portion until the base, surface, and cleanup are complete. Finally, require a written start date window and an estimated completion timeline given the interior seasonality.

Red flags we see in Kelowna: vague quotes with no base depth or drainage details, refusal to provide insurance/coverage documentation, “we’ll figure out the frost issues later” language, pressure to pay a large deposit upfront, and no written timeline or warranty specifics.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Kelowna

Do I need a permit to pave my driveway in British Columbia?

In most cases in British Columbia, repaving a private driveway within your own property footprint does not require a building permit. In Kelowna, the permit/approval question changes if you alter the municipal right-of-way—such as creating or changing a curb cut, modifying boulevard access, or affecting storm drainage. If you’re doing work that changes how water drains (for example, regrading that directs runoff toward the road) you should confirm with the city before excavation. Also, always call for utility locates via BC 1 Call before digging. A reputable contractor will ask these questions up front and will clearly state on the quote whether municipal approvals are needed.

How long before I can drive on new asphalt?

In Kelowna, most driveways can be used soon after the asphalt cools, but “ready to drive” depends on temperature, thickness, and weather. Typically, crews place and compact the hot-mix asphalt, then allow it to cool enough that it won’t deform under vehicle tires—often within a day under warm conditions. If the forecast is cool or damp, it can take longer. The bigger factor is avoiding rutting while the surface is still fragile. If you’re budgeting an asphalt job in the $4,500–$9,000 range, ask your contractor when they recommend your first seal coat; sealing is commonly done after the asphalt cures (often around 6–12 months).

What causes potholes and how are they repaired?

Potholes usually start from trapped water. In Kelowna and the Thompson–Okanagan, freeze–thaw cycles expand any water pockets under pavement, loosening the base and breaking the surface. Common triggers include cracked edges, poor drainage, an insufficient granular base, or traffic loading concentrated in one wheel path. Repairs typically involve cutting out the failed area to a sound depth, removing any saturated or loose material, re-compacting new granular base, and then placing asphalt patch material and compacting it to match elevation. For recurring failure, the fix may need broader base correction rather than a small patch. A good contractor will assess drainage and base conditions before just “filling the hole.”

How do I prepare for a paving project?

Preparation is where you protect your schedule and your final result. Start by clearing the driveway and garage area of vehicles, stored items and landscaping features within the work zone. If you have existing landscaping near edges, ask whether it will be protected or removed. Mark any utilities or sprinkler lines you’re aware of and ensure the property has safe access for equipment. Because utilities must be located, confirm the contractor will arrange BC 1 Call tickets and respect locate marks before excavation. Also, decide on drainage expectations: if you’ve noticed water pooling after rains, tell your contractor—those observations help justify base and grading work that prevents repeat freeze–thaw damage. Lastly, review the quote for what disposal and tear-out include.

How do I compare paving quotes fairly?

To compare quotes in Kelowna fairly, line items must match. Look for base depth and compaction expectations (not just surface thickness), granular material quality, and whether grading/drainage corrections are included. Confirm what tear-out and disposal are priced (breaking and hauling old asphalt or concrete can be a major cost driver). For asphalt, check whether the quote includes freeze–thaw-rated mix and whether sealing is included or scheduled (sealing is commonly recommended after 6–12 months). For pavers and concrete, compare edge restraints, bedding sand, jointing, and finish specifications. Also compare payment schedules, warranty wording, and the timeline. If one bid is far lower than others, ask what’s being omitted—often it’s base or drainage, which shows up later as cracking or settlement.

Can asphalt be laid in cold weather in Kelowna?

Asphalt in Kelowna needs suitable placement conditions. Hot-mix asphalt production and compaction generally require warm air temperatures and proper paving weather; crews typically won’t perform permanent asphalt paving when it’s too cold or when rain/ice risk is high. A practical rule used in the industry is that asphalt placement needs air temperatures around above 10°C for best results, with the pavement surface also needing to be workable. If it’s borderline cold, some contractors may postpone to protect compaction density and long-term durability—because freeze–thaw will punish weakly compacted areas. If you must work near the edge of the season, ask your contractor about weather risk, temporary protections, and how the base is kept dry.
