Driveway paving options and costs in Keating

Keating, British Columbia is a small community (population 1,403 as of the 2021 Census, Statistics Canada), but the driveway work is very “Lower Mainland–Southwest” in its demands: long wet seasons, heavy rain events, and frequent freeze–thaw activity around a relatively shallow frost line. In most homes here, driveways are attached to single-detached houses—so you’ll see a strong volume of residential replacement and upgrades, especially in established pockets like the Keating-area residential streets off the main corridors where drainage and curb access matter. Even when frost depth isn’t as severe as in the Interior, poor base preparation and drainage still drive the majority of failures: rutting, edge breakdown, and surface cracking that show up after a couple of winters.

For pricing, you’ll typically see asphalt starting lower than pavers or concrete, but asphalt performance depends heavily on granular base compaction and a sealing plan. Interlocking concrete pavers cost more upfront due to excavation, base, restraints and setting materials; they shine where you want a clean look and the ability to repair a section without replacing the whole driveway. Poured concrete can be a good mid-range option, but it must be properly air-entrained and jointed for Vancouver-area style freeze–thaw conditions.

What affects the price of paving in Keating

In Keating and across the Lower Mainland–Southwest, you can easily see driveway quotes swing by 30–50% for what looks like the same job. The biggest reason is that contractors price “site readiness” rather than just surface material: excavation volume, whether the base is rebuilt to spec, how drainage is corrected, and how much extra labour is needed to keep edges from failing when it rains hard. A second common driver is timing—work is booked tightly in spring and summer because hot-mix asphalt relies on air temperatures above about 10°C to place properly, and scheduling pressure can tighten lead times and labour availability.

Frost line depth still matters here, even though coastal BC is milder than Ontario/Alberta. Where deeper frost or weaker, clay-heavy subgrades are present, you need thicker compacted granular base (often in the 6–12 inch range in colder regions, with coastal BC commonly less than that when soils are stable). In parts of the Fraser Valley, saturated clay or poor drainage can act like “effective frost,” driving heaving and cracking. That’s why a well-built base and drainage strategy is what keeps a driveway within the typical asphalt band of $12 – $20 per sq ft rather than sliding toward “rebuild costs” after a couple of wet winters.

For concrete, costs can rise when contractors must add air-entrainment and control joints correctly, plus do more subbase stabilization. Pavers can reduce long-term replacement risk, but the edge restraints and bedding layer are labour-heavy—so if your quote starts in the paver range of $20 – $40 per sq ft, ask whether the contractor included proper excavation, bedding sand, and jointing.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property usually does not require a building permit. Most homeowners in Keating can replace a surface (for example, asphalt over a prepared base, or pavers/concrete on their lot) without a permit, as long as the work stays off municipal infrastructure and does not alter drainage leading to the right-of-way.

However, work may require municipal approval when it touches the municipal right-of-way or impacts how stormwater is managed. Typical examples include: new or modified curb cuts, changes to boulevard areas, altering side ditches or catch basins, rerouting or connecting drainage that discharges into the municipal system, or any grading that changes how water flows near the road or sidewalk. Some municipalities also enforce lot-grading bylaws that restrict how close a hard surface can sit to property lines or how water must be handled.

Step-by-step, here’s how a homeowner in Keating can verify a contractor properly in BC: (1) ask for their BC business/contractor licence info (where applicable for paving, the contractor should be transparent about their legal entity), (2) request a certificate of insurance showing they have liability coverage, and confirm the policy includes your address as an “additional insured” when your contract requires it, and (3) ask for proof of work coverage—workers’ compensation coverage documentation for their crews (coverage is commonly referenced in contracts as WSBC/WCB coverage). Then check the certificate expiry dates before any work begins, and keep copies with your contract and scope.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Keating driveways

For Keating driveways, all three surfaces can work—but they fail differently in the Lower Mainland–Southwest climate. Asphalt is the lowest upfront option and typically lands in the $12 – $20 per sq ft range when the base is properly rebuilt. Its key requirement is routine sealing every 3–5 years; without it, the binder weathers and rain water penetrates, accelerating cracking along wheel paths. Asphalt also tends to be more sensitive to poor edge drainage, which can matter during shoulder-season downpours.

Interlocking concrete pavers cost the most upfront, usually in the $20 – $40 per sq ft band, but they offer the best curb appeal and the most repair flexibility. If a driveway section needs attention due to settling or a drainage issue, pavers can be lifted, corrected, and reset—without tearing out the entire surface. That repairability is valuable where clayey soils or localized saturation show up after heavy rain.

Poured concrete is a mid-range choice (often around the $18 – $35 per sq ft band) but must be correctly air-entrained and jointed. Freeze–thaw movement in BC, combined with rain and occasional de-icing (where homeowners use salt near edges), can contribute to cracking if the mix and finishing aren’t right. Exposed aggregate improves slip resistance but still needs proper base and joints. In salt/plow-heavy driveways, maintenance discipline and joint spacing become the difference between “cracks in a few years” and “holds up much longer.”

Quick dollar example: if you compare asphalt at roughly $12 – $20 per sq ft versus pavers at about $20 – $40 per sq ft on a 400 sq ft driveway, the upfront paver spread can be several thousand dollars. That premium is justified when you value curb appeal, want easy repairs, and expect the site drainage needs attention over time.

How to choose a paving contractor in Keating

Choosing a contractor in Keating starts with verifying the right paperwork and then matching the quote to the real site conditions. In British Columbia, ask for proof of liability insurance—request a certificate of insurance and confirm it covers paving/excavation activities at your address. For crew coverage, verify they have the appropriate workers’ compensation coverage documents for their employees (coverage is commonly referred to under BC’s workers’ compensation system). Don’t accept “we’re insured” without documentation you can read: look for policy numbers, effective dates, and limits.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes that break down labour and materials (excavation, base gravel, bedding sand if pavers, asphalt/concrete, restraints, disposal, compaction testing if used, and sealing if asphalt). Avoid lump-sum quotes that don’t describe base depth, grading/drainage correction, or what will happen to existing edges and curb connections. Confirm whether the permit/approval process is included if your job affects the municipal right-of-way or storm drainage (even if a permit isn’t typical for private-lot paving, approval may still apply for curb cuts or drainage changes).

Warranty matters: ask for the workmanship warranty length in plain language, and whether product warranties apply to materials (and how they’re handled). Payment schedule should be conservative—typically never more than 10–15% upfront; hold back until key milestones are complete (especially base inspection and final surface cure/clean-up). Get a start date and a completion estimate in writing, along with weather contingencies because Lower Mainland–Southwest rain can pause asphalt and concrete placements.

Red flags in Keating: a contractor who won’t specify base depth or drainage steps; quotes that only compare surface thickness without addressing soil conditions; “cash only” or unclear payment terms; refusal to provide insurance/work coverage documents; and warranties that are vague (no time period or no workmanship details).

Frequently asked questions — paving in Keating

How long does a paved driveway last in Keating?

In Keating, driveway lifespan is mainly driven by base quality, drainage, and how well the surface is maintained through the wet season. With proper installation, asphalt driveways often last about 12–20 years, but that range assumes sealing is done regularly—typically every 3–5 years. Interlocking pavers can often reach 20–30 years because individual units can be lifted and reset if a section settles. Poured concrete, when air-entrained and correctly jointed, commonly falls in the 20–30 year range as well. If your site has clay-heavy soils or recurring puddling after storms, the “lasts longer” option becomes the one that fixes water first—because freeze–thaw movement around the shallow frost line can still create heaving even in milder coastal BC winters (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census for local population context).

When is the best time of year to pave in Keating?

For asphalt in Keating and the Lower Mainland–Southwest, late spring to early fall is usually the best window. Hot-mix asphalt placement depends on air temperatures (commonly above 10°C) so contractors can properly compact the mix and get a stable surface. Shoulder-season rain can also limit workable days, particularly after heavy storms when subgrades are saturated. For concrete, placement is easiest in dry, mild weather so finishing and curing can be controlled. If you’re comparing quotes, try to schedule base work and grading early, because the drainage and compaction stages set the foundation for everything that follows. Your contractor should provide a written schedule and note weather contingencies in the contract.

How deep should the base be for Keating's frost?

Keating’s frost is generally less intense than inland BC, but cost-effective longevity still requires building the base below the effective freeze–thaw zone for sensitive applications. In practice, most good residential asphalt jobs use about 6 inches of compacted granular base (with additional excavation and stabilization if soils are weak or saturated). If the subgrade is clayey or holds water, contractors may need to remove unsuitable material and replace with appropriate granular layers to prevent pumping, rutting, and heaving. The exact base depth should be stated in your quote based on the site’s soil and drainage observations. A contractor who won’t specify base depth is a problem—because frost line depth matters, but so does local saturation and drainage (Lower Mainland–Southwest drainage emphasis).

How often should I seal my asphalt driveway?

For Keating driveways, sealing is typically recommended every 3–5 years, depending on traffic and how much rain and UV exposure the surface gets. After a new asphalt placement, sealing is usually done after the pavement has properly cured—often after about 6–12 months. If you skip sealing, the binder can deteriorate faster, which shows up as surface raveling, fine cracking, and water penetration that accelerates edge failure. If you’re trying to budget, sealing is one of the least expensive maintenance steps relative to full replacement. When you receive a quote for asphalt around the $12 – $20 per sq ft band, ask whether sealing is included in the scope or if it’s a separate planned maintenance item.

Why does my driveway crack and heave in winter?

In Keating and much of the Lower Mainland–Southwest, cracking and heaving are often symptoms of water movement rather than “just frost.” When water infiltrates the driveway—through edges, low spots, or poorly drained subgrade—it can freeze during cold snaps and expand slightly. Repeated freeze–thaw cycles around a shallow effective frost line can lift weak sections, especially where soils are clay-heavy or where the base wasn’t compacted and drained correctly. Common triggers include inadequate slopes, missing edge restraints, and base materials that weren’t installed to the right thickness. If you’ve had visible puddling after rain, the fix is usually drainage and base rebuild, not just patching the surface. Patches can buy time, but persistent heave typically means the underlying water problem remains.

Do I need a permit to pave my driveway in British Columbia?

Typically, paving a private driveway within your own property in British Columbia doesn’t require a building permit. In Keating, many homeowners can replace an asphalt surface or install pavers/concrete without a permit, as long as you’re not changing municipal infrastructure. The work that usually can require municipal approval includes altering a curb cut, changing boulevard areas, or modifying storm drainage connected to the municipal right-of-way. Many municipalities also have lot-grading rules that restrict how water and hard surfaces must be handled near property lines. Before excavation, ensure utility locates are completed and confirm the municipal grading/drainage requirements. Your contractor should tell you clearly whether your scope triggers approval, and your contract should reflect who is responsible for that process.
