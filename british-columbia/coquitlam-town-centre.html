What affects the price of paving in Coquitlam Town Centre

In Coquitlam Town Centre, two quotes for the “same size driveway” can still differ by 30–50% across the Lower Mainland–Southwest and other parts of British Columbia because the price is driven by site conditions and the amount of base and drainage work required—not just the surface material you pick. Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property usually does not require a building permit. Typically, homeowners can replace or resurface the hard surface on their lot (for example, asphalt resurfacing or installing pavers) as long as it doesn’t alter municipal infrastructure. Where approvals commonly become necessary is when the work touches the municipal right-of-way—such as creating or changing a curb cut, altering the boulevard, or making changes that affect storm drainage flow into or through city infrastructure.

For Coquitlam Town Centre, always confirm any lot-grading or drainage-by-law requirements with the City before you start, especially if the project changes driveway elevations near property lines or modifies how water leaves your site. Another non-negotiable step in BC is utility locates before excavation. Call BC 1 Call (locating services) so your contractor can verify underground services before they dig.

To verify a contractor, ask for three things and check them directly: (1) British Columbia licence (look up contractor credentials in the applicable online registry), (2) liability insurance (request a current certificate of insurance showing your project is covered), and (3) WSBC/WCB coverage for workers (ask for proof of coverage or clearance documentation). A reputable paving company will provide these quickly and in writing before mobilization.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Coquitlam Town Centre driveways

For driveway projects in Coquitlam Town Centre, asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete each match different homeowner priorities in a Lower Mainland–Southwest climate where heavy rainfall and drainage matter as much as freeze–thaw performance. Asphalt usually wins on upfront cost and smoothness. It’s commonly in the $12–$20 per sq ft range when the base is built properly. The trade-off is maintenance: asphalt should be sealed after the initial cure window—often 6–12 months, then every 3–5 years—to protect against water damage and oxidation.

Interlocking concrete pavers have the highest typical upfront cost, often within the $20–$40 per sq ft band. In return, they’re easier to repair: if a section settles or a drainage issue is discovered, individual units can be lifted, corrected, and re-set without replacing the entire surface. That matters in Fraser Valley soils that can be clay-heavy in places and where saturation can cause base movement.

Poured concrete sits mid-range, but it must be done right for BC. Concrete needs proper base prep and, for exterior driveways, should be air-entrained to better tolerate freeze–thaw cycles. If you rely on de-icing salt and frequent plowing, the surface can still develop cracking over time unless the mix and detailing are correct.

Base preparation in this region still targets a frost-performance approach: residential asphalt often assumes about 6 inches of compacted granular base plus 2.5–3 inches of asphalt, with deeper excavation where soils are weak or saturated. If your current driveway has standing water or a soft subgrade, the added drainage and base work can justify choosing pavers (repairability) or concrete (durability) over asphalt-only savings. For example, if your asphalt quote is near $14–$16 per sq ft but your site needs significant base drainage corrections, the real cost gap to pavers narrows because both options require strong base layers.

How to choose a paving contractor in Coquitlam Town Centre

When you’re hiring for a driveway in Coquitlam Town Centre, confirm three coverage items before anyone digs: (1) British Columbia licensing, (2) liability insurance, and (3) WSBC/WCB coverage for workers. How to check: look up the contractor’s credentials in the relevant online registry for BC, then request a current certificate of insurance (COI) showing the contractor’s liability coverage is active and relevant to your work. For WSBC/WCB, ask for proof of coverage or a clearance letter before start-up. If a contractor can’t provide these promptly, that’s usually a sign of scheduling shortcuts or administrative gaps.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes that break out labour and materials (excavation, base gravel, compaction, asphalt lift or paver thickness, edging, geotextile if used, disposal, and cleanup). Insist on scope clarity: is permit pull included if your project affects the right-of-way, is disposal included for old concrete/asphalt, and what’s excluded (landscaping reinstatement, saw-cutting, curb modifications)?

Warranty matters in BC’s wet winters. Ask for the workmanship warranty length and whether it’s tied to proper base prep, drainage, and sealing timelines. Product warranties apply to materials, not installation. Also ask whether the warranty is transferable if you sell your home.

Finally, handle payment responsibly: never pay more than 10–15% upfront, and request a holdback until completion and final grade confirmation. Get a written timeline with a start date and an estimated completion date.
