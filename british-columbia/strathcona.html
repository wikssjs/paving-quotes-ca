Driveway paving options and costs in Strathcona

Strathcona homeowners typically choose between asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete because they balance appearance, maintenance, and performance in a wet coastal climate. With a population of about 12,585 (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census), Strathcona is a dense pocket where driveways are often shared along side yards and access can be tight—so contractors plan equipment and staging carefully. The surrounding Lower Mainland–Southwest market is also shaped by a large share of private households; in practical terms, most driveway work here serves attached to detached residential properties, which keeps demand steady for new installs and overlays throughout the milder seasons.

In Lower Mainland–Southwest, costs are driven more by intense rainfall and drainage than by deep frost, but we still design for freeze–thaw around the provincial frost-line assumptions used in regional practice. That means proper granular base thickness, compaction, and slope to move water away from the slab or asphalt surface are non-negotiable. Heavy winter rain can saturate weaker clay soils in parts of the Fraser Valley, and shoulder-season storms can compress paving windows—tightening scheduling and sometimes raising labour costs as crews get booked ahead.

In Strathcona, trade demand is especially noticeable around the older residential pockets and infill areas where lots are narrower and curbing/retaining details are common. What affects the price of paving in Strathcona

In Strathcona and the broader Lower Mainland–Southwest, two quotes for what looks like the “same” driveway can vary by 30–50% because the expensive parts are usually below grade: excavation limits, base design, drainage corrections, and how much of the existing surface must be removed. Asphalt may land anywhere in the typical $12–$20 per sq ft band when a contractor has to rebuild weak soils and fix grades; pavers can run higher in tight yards where restraints, edging, and careful base compaction take longer. In colder provinces, deeper freeze assumptions and shorter seasons can push asphalt and concrete pricing up; in coastal BC, frost isn’t as extreme, but wet winters and freeze–thaw around a shallow frost line still force a robust granular base and a well-draining section.

Freeze–thaw cycles are decisive cost factors in our region because repeated movement around the frost line can heave subgrade fines, especially where clay-heavy soils stay saturated. That’s why regional practice often targets around 6 in of compacted granular base plus a proper asphalt lift (commonly about 2.5–3 in for residential), with deeper excavation when soils are soft or saturated. In weak soil pockets, you may see that base thickness requirement jump and your quote can move into the upper end of the asphalt range. Asphalt mix design also matters; freeze–thaw-rated mixes and proper compaction are not optional.

Concrete costs can rise when the contractor must install air-entrainment and control joint layouts to manage cracking, and when disposal and demolition are significant. For example, tearing out old concrete or heavily deteriorated asphalt can add labour and hauling that isn’t apparent in first estimates. Conversely, if your existing base is stable and grades are correct, you can sometimes keep the project closer to the low end of the $18–$35 driveway concrete band.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property typically does not require a building permit. What often triggers municipal approval is anything that changes how water leaves your property or how the driveway interacts with the municipal right-of-way. As a homeowner in Strathcona, the work that usually does require approval includes new or modified curb cuts, changes to boulevard areas, alterations to storm drainage, and any changes that affect municipal infrastructure or how runoff is managed. Also keep in mind some municipalities regulate lot grading; if you’re planning to alter slopes close to property lines or raise/lower grades, you may need to follow lot-grading bylaws even if the driveway is on private land.

Before excavation, always arrange utility locates. In BC, the standard is to call BC 1 Call so underground services are marked—this reduces the risk of delays and damages. Step-by-step, you should also confirm your contractor’s credentials: (1) verify licensing through the BC government business/contractor listings where applicable for paving-related work, (2) request a current certificate of liability insurance showing coverage for the project, and (3) confirm workers’ compensation coverage (WCB) for the crew doing the work. Ask for clearance letters or proof documents rather than relying on verbal assurances, and keep copies in your project file for your own protection.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Strathcona driveways

For Strathcona driveways, asphalt is usually the lowest upfront option and gives a smooth finish, but it’s a maintenance product in wet coastal winters: you should plan on sealing every 3–5 years to protect the asphalt binder and slow water intrusion. Interlocking concrete pavers typically cost more up front, yet they’re highly repairable—if one area fails due to a localized drainage issue, a contractor can lift and reset affected units without replacing the entire driveway. Poured concrete sits in the middle for cost, but freeze–thaw movement and water saturation can still produce cracking over time if the concrete isn’t properly air-entrained and jointed.

Lower Mainland–Southwest’s climate means the driveway design still needs drainage-first thinking. Bases are commonly built with about 6 in of compacted granular base (and sometimes deeper excavation where soils are soft), and they’re graded so water doesn’t pond. Asphalt can perform well when that base is correct; pavers often tolerate minor movements better because individual units can be reset; concrete is sensitive to poor subgrade prep and to improper air-entrainment—especially when winter rains saturate the subgrade and then temperatures cycle around freezing.

Salt and sand from plowing can accelerate surface wear on all materials, but concrete without a good mix and finishing can spall or crack more noticeably. Here’s a realistic example: if an asphalt driveway is quoted around the middle of the $12–$20 per sq ft band and pavers land in the $20–$40 per sq ft band, the extra cost is most justified when curb appeal matters or when you want the option to repair small failed zones after a drainage correction.

How to choose a paving contractor in Strathcona

Choosing the right contractor matters as much as the material. In British Columbia, start by verifying that the company and crew are properly covered: ask for a current certificate of liability insurance (so damage to your property is covered), and confirm workers’ compensation coverage (WCB) for the workers who will be on-site. For licensing and standing, use online resources provided by the province and confirm the business is in good standing before work begins. Don’t accept a photo of a certificate—request a current copy that matches the company name you’re hiring.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes with a clear breakdown—labour, granular base materials, asphalt/concrete thickness, geotextile use (if specified), drainage/grading work, and disposal. Watch for exclusions like “no tear-out,” “no permit,” or “no additional excavation if base is poor,” because those are common drivers of surprise cost. A good contractor will also specify surface thickness (for example, asphalt lift thickness) and base requirements (compaction notes, granular depth, and how drainage will be handled).

Warranty should be explicit: workmanship warranty length, what product/manufacturer warranties apply, and whether they transfer to future owners. For payment, never pay more than 10–15% upfront; a holdback until completion is a sensible safeguard. Finally, insist on a written timeline with a start date and an estimated completion date.

  • Use a payment schedule that includes a substantial holdback until final grading and cleanup.

Red flags in Strathcona include: a quote that skips base thickness details, promises “cheap” paving without discussing drainage and soil condition, no proof of insurance/WCB, vague warranties (or no warranty at all), and a contractor pushing for large upfront payments without a written schedule.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Strathcona

How often should I seal my asphalt driveway?

In Strathcona and across British Columbia’s Lower Mainland–Southwest, most homeowners should plan to seal asphalt about every 3–5 years, assuming the driveway is draining well and you’re not dealing with heavy, constant water ponding. Sealing helps protect the asphalt binder from oxidation and reduces how quickly water penetrates the surface, which is important during our wet winter cycles. If the driveway is new, many contractors recommend waiting for proper curing before the first seal. After that, frequency depends on wear, sun exposure, and de-icing use. If your contractor quoted asphalt in the typical $12–$20 per sq ft band, sealing is often the best “small cost” step to protect that initial investment.

Why does my driveway crack and heave in winter?

Cracking and heaving usually come from water and soil movement—especially when water saturates the base, then temperatures cycle around freezing. Even though coastal BC has relatively shallow frost compared with Interior regions, freeze–thaw around a shallow frost line can still lift weak subgrade fines, particularly in areas with heavier clay soils or where drainage is inadequate. If you see cracking near edges, that often points to poor edge restraint or inadequate base support. Heaving in spring can also happen if the driveway isn’t graded to drain away from the house and into a proper outlet. The fix is rarely just surface patching; a contractor may recommend drainage correction and base rebuild where needed before resurfacing or overlay.

Do I need a permit to pave my driveway in British Columbia?

Typically, paving within your own property—like replacing asphalt on an existing driveway footprint—often does not require a building permit in British Columbia. However, municipal approval is commonly needed when the work affects the municipal right-of-way or storm drainage, such as new/changed curb cuts, boulevard modifications, or changes to how runoff flows. Some municipalities also have lot-grading rules that can limit how close or how high a hard surface can be near property lines. For Strathcona homeowners, the safest route is to ask your contractor to confirm what applies to your site and, if required, include the permit/approval steps in the quote. Always ensure utility locates are done before any excavation.

How long before I can drive on new asphalt?

After asphalt is installed in Strathcona, you can usually drive on it once it has cooled and compacted properly—often the same day for light traffic, but many contractors schedule around weather and equipment limitations. The key is avoiding tire pickup, ruts, or displacement while the asphalt is still vulnerable. Your contractor should specify a curing window in the written scope, especially if temperatures are cool or it’s forecast to rain. If you’re having a sealcoat applied as part of a later step, the driveway may need additional time before sealing and then before heavier use. As a rule of thumb, plan conservatively: follow your contractor’s guidance rather than trying to beat the schedule.

What causes potholes and how are they repaired?

Potholes are usually the result of water getting under the asphalt or through weak joints and then breaking down the base. In the Lower Mainland–Southwest climate, heavy rain can saturate the driveway, and freeze–thaw can widen the damage. Poor drainage, a lack of compaction, or existing low spots can accelerate failure. Repair typically starts with cutting out the damaged asphalt and removing any failed base material, then installing fresh granular base as designed before repaving and compacting the surface. For some driveways, a localized patch is enough; for broader deterioration, an overlay may be more cost-effective. If you’re already budgeting in the $12–$20 per sq ft asphalt range, a good quote should explain whether the repair is spot work or part of a wider surface strategy.

How do I prepare for a paving project?

Preparation is mostly about access, documentation, and decisions that prevent delays. In Strathcona, clear the driveway area of vehicles and landscaping features, and ensure gates and access paths are usable for delivery and compacting equipment. Keep sprinklers and any hose bibs protected. Before work begins, ask your contractor to show you the itemised plan: base thickness, thickness of asphalt or concrete, drainage/grading steps, disposal, and whether tear-out is included. Confirm insurance and WCB/WCB coverage is current, and request the written timeline. If you’re in a situation where municipal approval may be needed (like curb cut changes), ask for the plan to handle it. Lastly, schedule around weather and plan for the fact that shoulder-season rain can affect curing and scheduling in Lower Mainland–Southwest.
