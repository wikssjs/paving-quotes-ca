Driveway paving options and costs in Westridge

Westridge homeowners have several driveway surfacing options, and the “right” choice usually comes down to how your lot drains, how much traffic the driveway sees, and your tolerance for ongoing maintenance. Westridge’s population is small—2,521 residents (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census)—so trades can be busier during peak season, particularly around neighbourhood pockets where landscaping and curb appeal upgrades are common. In most homes, the driveway serves a detached house setting, which means private access driveways are the main demand driver.

In the Lower Mainland–Southwest, pricing is shaped more by intense rainfall, drainage quality, and shoulder-season storms than by deep Interior-style freezing. Even though coastal frost penetration is relatively shallow, regional practice still assumes a frost line on the order of 18" for sensitive infrastructure, so proper excavation, granular base, and slope/drainage detailing are where the money is usually won—or lost. That’s why two contractors can quote the same asphalt thickness but still land 30–40% apart once base depth, compaction testing, and disposal/haul-off are included.

If you’re weighing options, asphalt is typically the most budget-friendly to install, while interlocking pavers offer the best curb appeal and the most “replace-one-stone” flexibility. Concrete lands mid-to-upper depending on finish and whether you’re doing a full rebuild versus patching. What affects the price of paving in Westridge

In Westridge and across the Lower Mainland–Southwest, you can see the same driveway resurfacing scope come in 30–50% apart because base preparation and drainage details often differ between quotes, not just the surface material. In British Columbia, contractor availability and longer shoulder-season schedules can also influence labour rates and how quickly a job can start, which matters when rain compresses workable windows.

That affects scheduling and mix demand, especially when contractors are also doing curbs, drainage tie-ins, and other asphalt work.

Conversely, a straightforward, flat driveway on stable soils can come closer to the lower end of the $18–$35 band. Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway on your own property usually does not require a building permit. In most typical Westridge scenarios—removing an existing driveway surface and replacing it with asphalt or concrete within your driveway footprint—municipal approvals are often not triggered. However, you do need to watch for work that affects the municipal right-of-way.

Work that typically does require municipal approval includes new or altered curb cuts, changes to boulevard areas, and any rework that impacts storm drainage patterns (for example, redirecting runoff to an existing municipal catch basin, changing culverts, or modifying drainage within the road/ditch area). Some municipalities also have lot-grading bylaws that limit how close a hard surface can be to property lines or how water must be managed, so it’s wise to confirm grading rules before the crew mobilizes.

Step-by-step: how homeowners verify a contractor in BC

  • Request the contractor’s business/contractor licence details (and confirm they are active).
  • Ask for a current certificate of liability insurance; ensure it covers driveway paving and is not expired.
  • Confirm workplace coverage for workers through the applicable WCB system for construction (coverage should align with the work).
  • Do utility locates before digging: BC 1 Call (or the applicable utility locating system) should be used to mark buried services.
  • Before signing, confirm whether any permit or municipal approval is needed for curb/boulevard or drainage tie-ins.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Westridge driveways

For Westridge driveways, the “best” surface depends on freeze–thaw behaviour, how water drains in heavy rain, and how you maintain it through winter. Asphalt is usually the lowest upfront cost option to install, but it needs routine sealing every 3–5 years to protect the surface from wet-season damage and UV. In Lower Mainland–Southwest conditions, asphalt holds up well when the base is compacted and drainage is corrected, because failures often start below the surface where water can’t escape.

Interlocking concrete pavers cost more up front (often aligned with the $20–$40 band), yet they’re repair-friendly: if a utility trench opens later or a small section shifts, crews can lift and re-set pavers without replacing the entire driveway. That’s a big advantage in neighbourhoods where drainage and landscaping adjustments happen over time. Poured concrete sits in the middle cost range; it can last a long time, but without proper air-entrainment and jointing it may crack under freeze–thaw and winter salt exposure. Exposed aggregate offers traction and curb appeal, while still requiring good drainage and proper base to avoid movement.

Local winter maintenance matters too. With rain-snow mixes and winter sanding/salting routines, concrete and pavers tend to tolerate de-icing better than many unsealed asphalt surfaces, but asphalt can still perform well if sealed and if crack repairs are done promptly.

Where the price difference is justified: if your driveway is prone to localized settlement due to clay-heavy pockets, spending toward pavers can pay off because repairs are targeted. If your driveway is straightforward and drainage is addressed, asphalt around the $12–$20 range can be the smartest value, especially compared with full rebuilds in concrete or premium stone textures.

How to choose a paving contractor in Westridge

Choosing a paving contractor in Westridge is less about finding the cheapest quote and more about confirming you’re buying the process that prevents winter failures—excavation, base compaction, correct drainage, and proper surface thickness. In British Columbia, you should verify licensing status and insurance before any work starts. Ask for their certificate of liability insurance (make sure it’s current, and that it names you if required by your paperwork). For worker protection, confirm the contractor has the proper WCB coverage for the trades performing the work.

Get 2–3 itemised written quotes—this is critical. You want a breakdown that lists excavation depth, granular base type and thickness, asphalt or concrete thickness, any drainage/grading corrections, edge restraints/curbs, tear-out scope, and disposal. Avoid “lump sum” quotes that don’t show what’s included. Clarify whether permits (if any) are included, whether utility locates are arranged, and how they handle haul-off and site clean-up.

For warranty, separate workmanship from product/material warranties. Ask how long the workmanship warranty runs, whether it transfers to future owners, and what qualifies as “defect” versus normal wear. Payment schedules should be conservative: never pay more than 10–15% upfront, and hold back a portion until the final inspection and punch-list are completed. Also get a start date and an estimated completion timeline in writing—rain can delay asphalt placement because hot-mix placement generally needs air temperatures above about 10°C.

In Westridge, red flags include: quotes with no base depth or drainage details, “we’ll pave over it” language when soils appear saturated/soft, lack of insurance/coverage documentation, no written warranty terms, and crews that can’t explain schedule constraints for rain and asphalt temperature requirements.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Westridge

How deep should the base be for Westridge's frost?

For Westridge driveways, the practical answer is “deep enough to be below the influence of the frost line and resilient to freeze–thaw,” not just a number pulled from another province. In the Lower Mainland–Southwest, local practice still assumes an approximately 18" frost line for sensitive infrastructure, so the base and subbase need to be built with proper granular thickness and strong compaction, especially in clay-heavy pockets and areas that stay wet after rain. A common residential asphalt driveway build is about 6" of compacted granular base plus 2.5–3" of asphalt, with deeper excavation if the subgrade is soft or saturated. Your contractor should explain the excavated depth based on site conditions, not a one-size template. (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census)

How often should I seal my asphalt driveway?

In British Columbia, sealing is one of the most cost-effective protections for asphalt because our driveways see lots of wet-season exposure and temperature swings. Typically, you seal new asphalt after it has cured—often after 6–12 months—then plan on resealing about every 3–5 years depending on traffic, surface oxidation, and how quickly water begins to darken/soak the pavement. If you’re trying to budget, sealing can help asphalt last longer instead of forcing an earlier resurfacing. If your driveway is in the $12–$20 per sq ft range for installation, sealing is the small follow-up expense that protects that investment. If you skip sealing for many years, expect more frequent crack filling and earlier wear-through.

Why does my driveway crack and heave in winter?

Cracking and heaving usually point to movement within the base/subgrade. In Westridge, winter issues are often triggered by repeated freeze–thaw around a shallow frost influence level plus saturation from heavy rain. If drainage is poor (no slope crown, water collects at the edges, or downspouts discharge toward the driveway), water expands in colder periods and can lift weak spots—leading to heaving. If the granular base is too thin, poorly compacted, or not replaced when soils are soft, the surface can crack under driveway traffic. The good news: these failures are often preventable with the right excavation, drainage correction, and proper base thickness. That’s why quotes can differ significantly—base prep is usually the real driver of long-term performance.

Do I need a permit to pave my driveway in British Columbia?

Typically, paving a private driveway within your own property in British Columbia does not require a building permit. In Westridge, homeowners most often replace an existing driveway surface (asphalt, concrete, or pavers) within the driveway footprint, and that usually proceeds without a permit. Where approvals can be required is when your work affects the municipal right-of-way—like new or changed curb cuts, boulevard alterations, or storm drainage changes that tie into municipal infrastructure. Some areas also have lot-grading rules that restrict how water flows near property lines. Before excavation, also arrange utility locates through BC 1 Call and confirm any grading requirements with the municipality so you don’t run into delays after the base is already installed.

How long before I can drive on new asphalt?

Asphalt cure time depends on weather and mix type. In general, contractors allow vehicle traffic after the surface has cooled and achieved early stability—often the same day once it’s sufficiently cooled. However, for driveways, it’s common to restrict heavy turning, sharp stops, and parking on fresh asphalt for a period to reduce rutting or imprinting while the mix finishes curing. Rain timing matters in Westridge: spring and fall showers can delay placement, and wet conditions can affect compaction and surface acceptance. If you’re budgeting, compare the installation scope (base, thickness, and proper rolling) rather than only the $12–$20 per sq ft price band, because poor compaction can shorten the time you can safely drive and can reduce lifespan even if you can park quickly.

What causes potholes and how are they repaired?

Potholes usually form when water gets under the pavement and the base starts to soften or lose support. In Westridge, heavy rainfall plus freeze–thaw can accelerate this: water infiltrates through cracks or weak edges, then expands and loosens material. Traffic (especially turning loads near garages) enlarges the void until the surface breaks. Repair generally involves removing the failed asphalt to reach stable base, cleaning out loose material, and rebuilding with the correct granular base and asphalt thickness. A proper patch should be compacted and tapered to avoid abrupt edges. For long-term performance, crack sealing and keeping water from ponding are key—otherwise small repairs can come back. If your driveway is currently in the asphalt $12–$20 per sq ft installation range, targeted patching is still typically cheaper than waiting for full resurfacing.
