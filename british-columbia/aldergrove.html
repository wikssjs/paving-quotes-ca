Driveway paving options and costs in Aldergrove

Aldergrove homeowners typically choose between asphalt, interlocking concrete pavers, and concrete—each suited to the way Lower Mainland–Southwest winters behave. With a population of 12,363 (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census), the town has a steady mix of established residential neighbourhoods where driveways are getting refreshed, widened, or replaced for better drainage and curb appearance. Most private driveways serving single-detached homes (common across the area) create a strong demand for durable surfaces that can handle heavy rain events and the road “soak-and-dry” cycle that stresses weak base layers.

In the Lower Mainland–Southwest, costs are shaped more by intense rainfall and drainage than by deep frost. Frost penetration is relatively shallow, but local practice still assumes a frost line on the order of 18 inches for sensitive installations, so proper granular base thickness and compaction matter. That’s why two quotes for the “same driveway size” can differ: one contractor may excavate deeper, install thicker compacted base, and correct grading to keep water moving away from the house. Scheduling also plays a role—weather-wet shoulder seasons can compress workable windows for excavation and paving, affecting labour availability.

Demand is especially noticeable around commercial-facing and family residential pockets like Aldergrove’s busier main-access areas, where homeowners want a neat, long-lasting finish that stands up to ploughing and winter maintenance.

What affects the price of paving in Aldergrove

In Aldergrove and across the Lower Mainland–Southwest, it’s common to see the same driveway end up 30–50% apart between quotes—especially when one contractor prices for proper base and drainage and another keeps excavation to a minimum. The reason is that “paving cost” isn’t just asphalt or concrete: it’s excavation, granular placement, compaction, disposal, edge restraints, and the surface thickness needed to survive wet seasons and future repairs.

Frost line depth and freeze–thaw cycles are decisive cost factors even in coastal BC. While coastal frost is typically shallower than in Ontario or Alberta, local practice still assumes frost-sensitive conditions and therefore expects granular base thickness and proper drainage. If the base is too thin, repeated freeze–thaw around the shallow frost line can lead to heaving, rutting, and cracking. In colder regions, contractors commonly need 6–12 inches of compacted granular base; in coastal BC, it may be nearer 4–6 inches for some exposed areas, but clay-heavy spots in parts of the Fraser Valley can push the work deeper to stabilize the subgrade.

The paving season is also longer than Northern BC, but heavy winter rains and shoulder-season storms still shrink the number of days asphalt can be placed and compacted properly. Hot-mix asphalt placement generally needs air conditions above about 10°C, and mix design has to meet freeze–thaw durability expectations. That’s why a driveway budget at the lower end (for example, the general asphalt band around $12–$20 per sq ft equivalent scopes) can jump when thicker base, better drainage corrections, or tougher mix specifications are included.

Concrete can also vary: air-entrainment and correct finishing reduce surface spalling risks in British Columbia winters, while deep tie-in and saw-cut jointing add cost but help manage cracking. In a typical Aldergrove driveway replacement, adding 6 inches of base over weak areas may move you from “budget asphalt” thinking into a mid-range asphalt scope. For homeowners comparing materials, the jump from basic asphalt pricing into paver pricing is often justified by the long-term repairability and the need for curb restraint in wetter yards.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, most paving on your own property usually doesn’t require a building permit. That typically includes resurfacing a driveway entirely within your lot and replacing asphalt/concrete or interlocking pavers where you are not changing the connection to the municipal right-of-way. However, work in the municipal right-of-way generally does require approval—especially if you’re requesting a new curb cut, altering the boulevard, changing storm drainage patterns, or modifying how water flows toward the street.

Some municipalities also have lot-grading or drainage expectations that limit how close hard surfaces can be to property lines and how runoff is directed. Before anyone starts digging, confirm lot-grading rules with the local municipality, and request utility locates. In BC, call BC 1 Call (or your local utility locate process) so excavators can verify buried services before base excavation.

For a homeowner in Aldergrove, verifying a contractor’s credentials is straightforward. Ask for their BC contractor licence details (and confirm on the appropriate online registry). Request a certificate of liability insurance naming you and confirm the policy limits match the scope. For workers and subcontractors, confirm coverage through WorkSafeBC (WCB)—you want proof that the contractor is in good standing. Finally, ask whether they provide a clearance letter or proof of coverage for the project period. A reputable paving crew won’t hesitate to provide these documents before scheduling excavation.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Aldergrove driveways

For Aldergrove driveways, asphalt usually wins on lowest upfront cost, pavers win on curb appeal and “repairability without full replacement,” and concrete sits in the middle—durable when properly designed, but more sensitive to freeze–thaw and de-icing practices if it’s not air-entrained and correctly finished. In the Lower Mainland–Southwest, heavy rainfall and drainage control matter as much as frost depth. A good asphalt driveway typically uses around 6 inches of compacted granular base with about 2.5–3 inches of asphalt on top, while pavers generally require 6 inches of base plus bedding sand and 2.5–3.5 inches of pavers. If the subgrade is weak or saturated, excavation depth increases, and that’s where costs move fast.

Salt and winter maintenance also influence the choice. With frequent ploughing and traffic, interlocking pavers can be easier to fix: if a small area shifts due to a drainage issue, a contractor can remove and re-set affected units. Asphalt can also be patched, but repeated moisture problems often show up as potholes where base drainage is weak. Poured concrete should be air-entrained and jointed; otherwise, freeze–thaw can contribute to surface cracking over time.

Here’s a real budgeting example: if asphalt lands in the $12–$20 paving band for typical driveway scopes, pavers often come out higher—frequently near the $20–$40 interlocking range—yet the extra cost can be justified when you want a long-lived, high-appearance surface and the ability to target repairs instead of replacing the whole driveway. When drainage is corrected and the base is built properly, each material can perform well; when it isn’t, even the “best” surface can fail early.

How to choose a paving contractor in Aldergrove

Choosing the right paving contractor in Aldergrove comes down to proof and details. In British Columbia, verify the contractor’s business and licensing information through the appropriate online registry, then request a certificate of liability insurance with adequate limits for the job and your property address. For worker coverage, confirm WorkSafeBC (WCB) coverage and ask for confirmation that subcontractors are also covered. If they can’t provide documents promptly, that’s your first sign to pause.

Next, request 2–3 itemised written quotes that break out labour and materials, not just a lump sum. You want line items for excavation, base supply, compaction, asphalt/pavers/concrete, edge restraints, disposal, and any drainage or grading correction. Ask what’s excluded: permit pull (if any right-of-way work is involved), traffic control, protection of landscaping, and whether tear-out is fully included. Also confirm whether they include disposal of spoils and how they handle hauling restrictions.

Warranty matters. Ask for the workmanship warranty length, whether product warranties apply to material supply, and whether warranties are transferable if you sell your home. For payment, never agree to more than about 10–15% upfront. A holdback until substantial completion is standard and protects you if something needs to be re-done. Make sure the quote includes a start date target and completion estimate in writing—weather delays can happen, but the plan should be clear.

Red flags I see too often in Aldergrove: crews who won’t show insurance/WCB paperwork, quotes that skip base thickness and compaction details, “overlay” pitches without confirming base stability, vague drainage language (no slope plan), and warranties that cover only materials while excluding workmanship.

What base thickness do I need for pavers in Aldergrove?

In Aldergrove, paver performance depends on both base thickness and drainage. A common residential specification is about 6 inches of compacted granular base, topped with bedding sand, then 2.5–3.5 inches of pavers. If your yard has clay-heavy soil or signs of saturation (soft spots after rain), the excavation often needs to go deeper so the base is built on stable material. Even though coastal frost is relatively shallow, local practice still targets frost-sensitive conditions, so the contractor should ensure the base and drainage prevent water accumulation that can stress the structure. Ask your contractor to describe the base depth, compaction method, and how they’ll manage slope away from the house (no ponding near foundations).

How do I get the most accurate paving quote in Aldergrove?

To get a quote that you can compare apples-to-apples in Aldergrove, ask for an on-site measurement and a written, itemised scope. The biggest accuracy boost is for the contractor to confirm actual excavation depth and base condition (especially if you have old gravel or deteriorating asphalt). Make sure the quote lists excavation, disposal, base materials, compaction, and the finished surface thickness—those are the items that swing costs by 30–50% across the Lower Mainland–Southwest. Clarify whether tear-out is included, whether edge restraints are included, and whether they’re allowing for drainage correction. If you’re considering asphalt, confirm the sealing plan and timing; a typical asphalt driveway scope often lands in the $12–$20 pricing band depending on base and drainage work.

How much does it cost to pave a driveway in Aldergrove?

Driveway paving costs in Aldergrove typically fall into the regional bands based on material and how much base/grading work is required. For asphalt driveway paving, many residential projects land in the general $12–$20 range (per sq ft equivalent scope), while interlocking pavers more commonly land in the $20–$40 range because of base prep, bedding sand, paver quantity, and edge restraints. Concrete driveway pricing often sits in the $18–$35 range depending on whether you choose broom finish or exposed aggregate and how much excavation is needed. If your existing base is unstable, the final number can rise because additional granular depth and drainage improvements are often necessary in Fraser Valley clay areas.

Asphalt vs interlocking pavers vs concrete — which is best in Aldergrove?

“Best” depends on how you value upfront cost versus long-term appearance and repair flexibility in Aldergrove. Asphalt is usually the best value upfront and works well when the base and drainage are done correctly; plan to seal every 3–5 years. Interlocking concrete pavers are often the best choice if curb appeal is a priority and you want the ability to repair localized areas without replacing the whole surface—though initial cost is higher (often the $20–$40 band). Poured concrete can be a strong, solid option in BC, but it needs air-entrainment, correct jointing, and a proper base to limit freeze–thaw cracking. For most homeowners, the deciding factor is drainage quality: a well-built base makes all three systems perform.

How long does a paved driveway last in Aldergrove?

Typical service life for Aldergrove driveways depends on construction quality, drainage, and maintenance. Asphalt commonly lasts about 15–25 years when installed over a properly compacted base and sealed regularly (often every 3–5 years). Interlocking pavers often perform for 25–35 years because individual pavers can be reset if minor shifting occurs from seasonal moisture. Poured concrete is commonly 20–30 years when air-entrained and built with proper joints and base support. Expect shorter life if water pools, if base thickness is reduced to cut costs, or if sealing is neglected for asphalt. Because Lower Mainland–Southwest winters bring heavy rainfall, drainage and slope away from the house are the difference between “good life” and early failures.

When is the best time of year to pave in Aldergrove?

The best time for paving in Aldergrove is late spring through summer, when weather is more predictable for excavation, compaction, and surface installation. Asphalt placement generally requires air temperatures above about 10°C, and crews also need dry conditions to keep base material from becoming oversaturated during compaction and placement. Even though the season is longer than in Northern BC, heavy winter rains and shoulder-season storms can delay work—so scheduling early helps. If you’re planning a full replacement, aim for a period where the contractor can complete base prep, surface installation, and any required curing/sealing steps without rushing. Your contractor should also include weather contingencies in writing rather than relying on verbal “we’ll see” scheduling.
