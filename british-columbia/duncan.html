Driveway paving options and costs in Duncan

Duncan homeowners often start with one question: what will a new, long-lasting driveway actually cost? In a town shaped by a lot of detached housing—single-detached homes make up 41.8% of dwellings—most private driveways are attached to detached properties, so the market demand stays steady for upgrades on everything from 10-year-old asphalt to older gravel approaches. With 61.3% of homes built before 1981, it’s also common to see mature driveways where base issues, poor drainage, and settlement show up as soon as you excavate.

On Vancouver Island and the Coast, paving is typically cheaper than the far north because our coastal climate is milder, but Duncan still deals with frost pockets and repeated freeze-thaw cycles. Around Saanich, Nanaimo, and the broader Island network, we see that “shallow frost” doesn’t mean “no frost”—it still drives heaving and cracking when the granular base and drainage aren’t engineered. Pricing can also move based on seasonal availability: the paving season can tighten when shoulder months are wetter and colder, and trucking and labour costs can add an Island premium versus some mainland regions.

If you want predictable budgeting, start by choosing a material, then expect scope differences for excavation, haul-off, and base work. For many homes in North Duncan and near the downtown/Butchart Gardens-bound commuter corridor, driveway replacements are especially in demand after drainage problems or surface breakdown. What affects the price of paving in Duncan

If you’re seeing driveway quotes that don’t match, it’s not unusual for the same basic driveway size to land 30–50% apart in cost across Vancouver Island and Coast and the rest of British Columbia. The spread usually comes down to what’s happening under the surface: excavation depth, how much drainage correction is required, whether the contractor needs to rebuild poor soils, and how accessible the site is for skid-steers and dump trucks.

On Vancouver Island and the Coast, frost line depth and freeze-thaw cycles are decisive cost factors. While the frost line is relatively shallow compared with colder Canadian regions, Duncan still experiences localized freezing and repeated freeze-thaw. That means we often build a properly compacted granular base (commonly in the 4–6 inch range for well-prepped coastal sites, and more if the existing subgrade is unstable) to prevent heaving and cracking. In a deeper-frost zone you might see 6–12 inches of granular base used more often; on the Island we can sometimes stay thinner—but only when drainage and compaction are right.

Concrete examples in Duncan: (1) older driveways from the pre-1981 housing stock may have limited subgrade support; more base rebuild increases concrete costs quickly. (2) If runoff is spilling toward the foundation or down a slope that needs regrading, drainage work can add significant labour regardless of whether you choose asphalt (often around $10–$20 per sq ft) or interlocking pavers (commonly $25–$45 per sq ft). Asphalt also has scheduling pressure—hot-mix production and paving require conditions that typically align better in clearer windows, and mix design has to suit local freeze-thaw performance. When a contractor has to hold material, rebook crews, or haul extra aggregate for reconstruction, pricing reflects it.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property typically does not require a building permit. In most Duncan driveway projects, the work is confined to the lot—removing the existing surface, rebuilding base, and installing asphalt, pavers, or concrete—so municipal approvals are often not required. However, work that touches the municipal right-of-way does require approval. That includes new or modified curb cuts, changes to the boulevard, altering storm drainage, or any work that impacts municipal drainage infrastructure.

Some areas also regulate grading through lot-grading bylaws, which can restrict how close you can place hard surfaces to property lines and how runoff must be directed. Because Duncan projects often involve drainage corrections near the road frontage, it’s smart to confirm lot-grading rules with the municipality before excavation.

Before any digging, always call for utility locates through BC 1 Call. Step-by-step, a homeowner can also verify contractor credentials in a way that actually reduces risk:

  • Ask for proof of British Columbia business registration/licence where applicable (contractor details should be clear on your quote).
  • Request a current certificate of liability insurance naming you as applicable (or confirming it covers property damage).
  • Confirm worker coverage: ask for WSBC/WCB clearance or account details and verify the status letter/certificate.
  • Confirm the licence/coverage names match the contracting entity on the proposal and invoice.
  • Keep copies—screenshots of online registry results plus the certificate dates.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Duncan driveways

For Duncan’s freeze-thaw reality, the “best” driveway is the one that pairs the right base and drainage with a surface system you can maintain. Asphalt is usually the lowest upfront choice, and it offers a smooth drive. The trade-off is maintenance: in coastal BC, asphalt should be sealed after installation (often within the first 6–12 months depending on conditions) and then resealed about every 3–5 years to protect the binder from moisture and wear. When sealed on schedule, asphalt commonly performs well through Island winters; when it isn’t, raveling and surface breakdown can show up sooner.

Interlocking concrete pavers cost more up front (often in the $25–$45 per sq ft range), but they handle Duncan’s localized movement better because individual units can be lifted and reset without replacing the whole driveway. Pavers also excel where you want clean curb appeal and tight control at edges.

Poured concrete is mid-range and can last a long time, but it is more susceptible to cracking if the slab isn’t properly designed and air-entrained for freeze-thaw. Exposed aggregate offers a higher-end look, while permeable drainage approaches can help when water management is a challenge.

Budget example: if an asphalt driveway comes in around the $10–$16 per sq ft range, moving to interlocking pavers can add roughly $8–$25 per sq ft in many Duncan projects once labour, restraints, and edge work are included. That difference is usually justified when you want maximum curb appeal, easy future repairs, and a surface that can tolerate spot fixes without downtime. In contrast, if your primary goal is lowest initial cost and you’re comfortable with regular sealing, asphalt is often the smartest fit.

How to choose a paving contractor in Duncan

Choosing a contractor in Duncan is mostly about verifying risk control and making sure the scope matches the performance you want in a freeze-thaw climate. Start with credentials: in British Columbia, require proof of proper liability insurance (and that it covers the work described), then confirm worker coverage through WSBC/WCB. Don’t just accept a sentence in an email—ask for the certificate details (company name, effective date, and coverage limits) and ensure the contracting entity matches what’s on the quote and invoice. If you use the contractor again later, having up-to-date paperwork prevents coverage gaps.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes. You want a breakdown that separates labour from materials (asphalt, base, geotextile if used, restraints/edges, removal/haul-off, and compaction). Avoid single lump-sum quotes that don’t explain what’s included. Scope questions to ask: is permit/municipal approval administration included if you need right-of-way changes, is disposal included for concrete/asphalt, and are you getting replacement topsoil at disturbed areas? For driveway grading, confirm how they handle drainage—small mistakes can cause water to sit under the surface and speed up freeze-thaw damage.

Warranty matters: ask for workmanship warranty length and whether it covers labour only or labour plus materials. Product/manufacturer warranties apply to specific materials (not base construction). Also confirm whether warranties are transferable if you sell the home.

Payment schedule should be sensible: never pay more than 10–15% upfront. Hold back the remaining balance until the job is complete and the surface is accepted. Finally, require a written start date and completion estimate, not just “weather permitting.” On Vancouver Island, rain and soil saturation can delay baserock and paving, so timelines should be realistic.

In Duncan, some common red flags include: quotes that don’t specify base thickness or drainage corrections, refusing to provide insurance/coverage paperwork, using “we’ll patch it later” as a plan for failing base, starting without a utility locate confirmation, and offering the lowest price while skipping edge restraints, sealing timelines, or proper compacted road base.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Duncan

What is a chip seal driveway and is it suited to Duncan?

Chip seal (also called tar-and-chip) is a surface treatment where a binder is sprayed and aggregate is broadcast, creating a textured wearing layer. In British Columbia it can be suitable for some properties—especially where lower cost and quick installation matter—but it’s not the best fit for every Duncan driveway because localized freeze-thaw and water movement can loosen aggregate if the base and drainage aren’t engineered. If your driveway sees frequent standing water, or your current base is unstable, chip seal may deteriorate faster than asphalt or properly built pavers. Pricing is often lower than new asphalt, but performance depends heavily on prep. As a rough budgeting anchor, asphalt driveway paving commonly runs about $10–$20 per sq ft, so chip seal may start below that while still requiring maintenance and eventual re-treatment.

How much does driveway crack repair cost in Duncan?

Crack repair pricing in Duncan usually depends on how wide the cracks are, whether water is entering the base, and whether you’re sealing a few small areas or doing a larger patch program. For tight, stable cracks, contractors may price crack cleaning and sealing as a small-line repair (often comparable to a “minimum charge” service). If cracks have already opened due to freeze-thaw movement, sealing alone may not solve the problem and you may need partial removal and asphalt patching. As a practical range, many homeowners see driveway repair budgets starting around the $1–$4 band noted for sealing and repair work, but larger patch jobs can increase quickly once base stabilization is required. A good contractor should diagnose drainage and base condition before quoting a final repair plan.

What base thickness do I need for pavers in Duncan?

In Duncan, paver performance hinges on the base and drainage more than the paver type. Because Vancouver Island and the Coast have a relatively shallow frost line, you often don’t need the very deep granular bases used in colder interior regions, but you still must account for localized freezing and freeze-thaw cycles. For many well-drained coastal sites, a properly compacted granular base in the general 4–6 inch range is common when the subgrade is stable; if the subgrade is soft, saturated, or the existing driveway is failing, contractors may need to excavate deeper and rebuild to achieve the right compaction. The exact thickness should come from a site assessment—especially around slopes and driveway/sidewalk transitions—because water sitting under the bedding layer is what accelerates movement and joint failure.

How do I get the most accurate paving quote in Duncan?

To get a quote you can compare apples-to-apples in Duncan, start with an on-site visit and ask for itemised pricing. Request a breakdown separating excavation/tear-out, base and compaction materials, asphalt or concrete supply, edging/restraints, and disposal/haul-off. Make sure the contractor measures driveway area accurately (square footage) and documents site access constraints that affect labour and truck movement. Ask how they will handle drainage and driveway slope so freeze-thaw doesn’t keep turning water into the enemy. Also confirm whether any right-of-way changes (curb cuts, boulevard modifications, storm drainage adjustments) are included or excluded in the price. Finally, ask for a written start date and completion window that reflects coastal weather—because scheduling in wetter shoulder periods can change costs.

How much does it cost to pave a driveway in Duncan?

Driveway paving costs in Duncan vary mainly by square footage, tear-out needs, base rebuild depth, and access. As a local budgeting guide, asphalt new installation commonly lands in the $10–$20 per sq ft range, while interlocking concrete pavers are often in the $25–$45 per sq ft range; poured concrete is frequently $18–$35 per sq ft depending on finish. Sealing and repairs for asphalt are typically budgeted separately, with many projects falling into the $1–$4 range for sealing/repair work depending on what’s actually needed. If your existing driveway has poor drainage or weak subgrade (common on older properties), excavation and base rebuild can shift the final total significantly. Ask your contractor to provide an itemised quote so you can see exactly how much is being spent on base and drainage versus surface material.

Asphalt vs interlocking pavers vs concrete — which is best in Duncan?

In Duncan and across Vancouver Island and the Coast, all three can work when the base and drainage are engineered for freeze-thaw conditions. Asphalt is usually the best choice for lowest upfront cost and a smooth ride, but it requires sealing regularly (commonly every 3–5 years after the initial protection period) to hold up. Interlocking concrete pavers cost more, but they’re often the best “repairable” option—if a section shifts, individual units can be lifted and reset without replacing the entire driveway. Poured concrete sits in between: it can last a long time if air-entrained and properly designed, but it can crack if freeze-thaw movement isn’t managed by the base and slab detailing. If you’re trying to stretch value, choose based on how much maintenance you’re willing to do and whether you want future repairs without full demolition.
