Driveway paving options and costs in Seaview

In Seaview, British Columbia, you’ll usually be choosing between asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete—each works differently with our Lower Mainland–Southwest rainfall and drainage patterns. With a 2021 population of 3,236 (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census), the local market is smaller than Vancouver, so the best pricing often comes when contractors can group jobs efficiently in nearby streets and cul-de-sacs. Also, Seaview’s housing stock is largely made up of private homes that generate steady demand for driveways—especially in the older-residential pockets where access roads were built before modern drainage standards.

In the Lower Mainland–Southwest, frost penetration isn’t as extreme as in Ontario or Alberta, but you still need to plan for freeze–thaw around a shallow frost line. That’s why contractors in this region focus heavily on proper granular base depth, compaction, and edge restraint. The rainy shoulder seasons can also squeeze the paving schedule: if the subgrade stays wet, excavation and base placement take longer, and asphalt placement windows tighten.

Because of that, two quotes for “the same driveway size” can differ based on whether the contractor includes granular base upgrades, drainage corrections, and proper asphalt thickness. What affects the price of paving in Seaview

In Seaview and across the Lower Mainland–Southwest, it’s normal to see the same driveway project land 30–50% apart in price between contractors. The biggest reason isn’t “overhead”—it’s whether the quote properly accounts for what our coastal climate demands: drainage correction, the right granular base thickness, and enough asphalt/concrete thickness to stand up to repeated wetting, drying, and freeze–thaw at a relatively shallow frost line.

Frost line depth and freeze–thaw cycles are decisive cost factors. In colder regions, contractors often build thicker bases (6–12 inches in many Interior or prairie-like applications) because deep freezing increases heave risk. In coastal BC, the freeze depth can be shallower, but good practice still targets a robust, compacted base so the subgrade doesn’t pump water upward and crack the surface. That’s why Lower Mainland–Southwest quotes may still include base work that surprises homeowners who expect “milder winter” means “less base.” Asphalt mix design matters too: freeze–thaw-rated mixes cost slightly more, and that cost shows up inside the typical asphalt range of $12 – $20 per sq ft for properly built driveways.

Concrete costs can swing depending on thickness, reinforcement/air entrainment, and whether the contractor allows for heavier finishing and curing time. In Seaview, two common examples: (1) if your soil is clay-heavy and holds water, base excavation and drainage work can add more labour than the concrete itself; (2) if the driveway has tight access near a narrow lane, unloading and compaction equipment takes longer, pushing labour upward even if the square footage is the same. In either case, older driveways often need tear-out and hauling, and tear-out work is where “cheap” estimates quickly catch up to realistic pricing—often pulling paving closer to the $18 – $35 band for poured concrete or the $20 – $40 band for pavers.

Permits & regulations in British Columbia

In British Columbia, paving a private driveway within your own property usually doesn’t require a building permit. However, the permit/approval question changes if the work touches the municipal “right-of-way.” In practice, that means new curb cuts, modifications to the boulevard area, changes to storm drainage, altering municipal catch basins/ditches, or any work that affects how surface water leaves the road and property typically needs municipal approval before construction.

Some municipalities also have lot-grading bylaws that restrict how close a hard surface (and sometimes how much grading) can be to property lines. Before you sign a contract, confirm whether your proposed grade ties into existing swales correctly and whether any drainage corrections fall under municipal oversight. Utility locates are also essential: call BC 1 Call before excavation to mark buried lines and reduce the risk of service strikes.

To verify a Seaview paving contractor in British Columbia, ask for three items before work starts: (1) licence/registration information where applicable for the contractor’s business profile (check the contractor’s online presence/registration details), (2) liability insurance—your “Certificate of Insurance” should list you as an additional insured if the contract requires it, and (3) Workers’ coverage—for work in BC, confirm the contractor has current provincial workers’ compensation coverage. Request the clearance letter or proof of coverage, and confirm the certificate is not expired. Then match the legal business name on the insurance documents to the name on the quote and contract.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Seaview driveways

For Seaview homeowners, asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete all can perform well, but each comes with trade-offs that matter in our Lower Mainland–Southwest conditions—constant moisture, drainage sensitivity, and freeze–thaw near a relatively shallow frost line. Asphalt is typically the lowest upfront cost and gives a smooth, quiet ride; the catch is you need a maintenance plan, especially sealing every 3–5 years after the initial curing period. When the base is built right (granular layers, proper slope, and compaction), asphalt is often the best “value-per-year,” commonly aligning with the regional asphalt band of $12 – $20 per sq ft.

Interlocking concrete pavers have the highest upfront cost in most cases, but they’re also the most repairable. If a utility job happens later or if one spot experiences settlement, pavers can be lifted, re-based, and reset—without tearing out the entire driveway. That repair-friendly advantage is why many Seaview homeowners pick pavers along high-visibility entrances where curb appeal matters and small localized repairs are worth the premium (often in the $20 – $40 range).

Poured concrete sits mid-range in cost and can last a long time, but it’s more susceptible to cracking if the base isn’t uniform or if air entrainment and proper finishing aren’t done correctly for freeze–thaw. If you use de-icing salts in winter, surface wear can be more noticeable unless the mix is appropriate and curing is handled well. For a practical example: on a 400 sq ft driveway, choosing pavers over asphalt can look like a gap of roughly $4,000 – $9,000 depending on base and restraints—worth it when you want long-term curb appeal and easy spot repairs, but not necessary if your priority is lowest upfront cost and you’re willing to seal and maintain asphalt.

How to choose a paving contractor in Seaview

When you’re hiring a paving contractor in Seaview, don’t rely on a nice website or a low initial number. In British Columbia, ask directly about (1) licensing/registration for the business, (2) liability insurance, and (3) workers’ compensation coverage. To check: request a Certificate of Insurance (COI) showing current dates and adequate liability limits, and confirm the legal business name on the COI matches the quote. For workers’ coverage, ask for proof/clearance documentation and ensure it covers the timeframe of your project. If any document is expired or doesn’t match the company you’re hiring, that’s a stop sign.

Get 2–3 itemised written quotes instead of a lump sum. A proper quote breaks down labour and materials line-by-line: excavation and disposal, granular base (including depth), compaction, drainage adjustments, asphalt/concrete thickness, edges/restraints, and any surface prep. Read the scope carefully: what’s excluded (topsoil reinstatement, off-haul, curb cut approvals, disposal), and is permit/municipal approval work included if required? Warranty details matter too—ask for workmanship warranty length, what’s covered (settlement vs surface cracking), and whether the manufacturer warranty transfers to you if materials are involved.

For payment, never pay more than about 10–15% upfront. Use a holdback until the job is complete and graded correctly. Finally, get start date and completion estimate in writing, with contingencies for rainy scheduling windows typical in our coastal weather.

  • Ask for and review the exact scope of excavation and base depth (in inches) for your soil and drainage.
  • Confirm disposal is included (haul away) if tear-out is part of the job.
  • Require drainage notes: slope direction, water management, and any swales/pipe work.
  • Check that edge restraints/curbs are included to prevent paver spread or asphalt edge failure.
  • Get asphalt thickness or concrete thickness stated clearly in the quote.
  • Ask whether concrete is air-entrained and how curing is handled.
  • Confirm sealing timing for asphalt (typically after curing, often 6–12 months).
  • Request warranty terms in plain language and what constitutes “defect” vs “maintenance.”
  • Verify insurance certificates are current and list jobsite coverage.
  • Confirm workers’ compensation proof/clearance is current for the contractor’s crews.
  • Ensure there’s a written schedule and a plan for rainy-season delays.
  • Avoid contractors who won’t provide itemised quotes or won’t put scope exclusions in writing.

Red flags in Seaview include: refusing to provide COIs or workers’ coverage proof, quoting without stating base depth and thickness, vague timelines with no written start/completion dates, “cash only” pressure to pay large deposits upfront, and omitting drainage corrections while still promising a long lifespan.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Seaview

How deep should the base be for Seaview's frost?

In Seaview and the Lower Mainland–Southwest, you typically don’t build for deep Interior frost, but you still need a well-compacted granular base to handle freeze–thaw near the shallow frost line and to stop water from pumping upward. For most residential driveway builds, local practice commonly uses about 6 inches of compacted granular base under asphalt, with additional excavation in saturated or weak soils. If drainage is poor or soil is clay-heavy, the contractor should recommend deeper or upgraded base and include drainage corrections in the scope. The exact depth should be tied to site conditions—so when you compare quotes, insist the contractor states the base thickness in inches and describes how they’ll manage water on and under the driveway.

How often should I seal my asphalt driveway?

For asphalt in British Columbia, sealing is mainly about protecting the binder from moisture and oxidation in wet coastal conditions. Most homeowners should plan on sealing every 3–5 years after the driveway has properly cured. If you recently installed new asphalt, sealing is often deferred until the surface is ready—commonly after 6–12 months—so the sealer bonds well instead of trapping moisture. After that, reseal at the interval recommended by your contractor based on traffic and how much water gets to the edges. If your driveway is built right for drainage, you’ll typically get more consistent performance and fewer “early” repairs.

Why does my driveway crack and heave in winter?

In Seaview, winter cracking and heaving usually comes down to water and base conditions, not just temperature. If the subgrade holds water—common where clay soils drain slowly—freeze–thaw cycles can form ice lenses near the frost-affected zone and lift the pavement slightly. Asphalt may then crack, and concrete may show cracking or uneven edges. Another cause is insufficient base thickness or inadequate compaction, especially at transitions like garage aprons or driveway edges where water concentrates. The remedy is rarely “just patching.” A contractor should assess drainage, check slopes, and confirm granular base depth and compaction, then recommend repairs before resurfacing—otherwise the same failure mode can reappear.

Do I need a permit to pave my driveway in British Columbia?

Typically, paving within your own property footprint usually doesn’t require a building permit in British Columbia. Where you’ll need municipal approval is when the work impacts the municipal right-of-way—examples include creating or changing a curb cut, altering the boulevard, or changing storm drainage features near the road. Seaview homeowners should also check any local lot-grading bylaws that can affect how close or how high a hard surface can be to property lines and how runoff must be managed. Before excavation, confirm utility locates through BC 1 Call. Your contractor should tell you whether your scope touches the right-of-way so you’re not surprised later.

How long before I can drive on new asphalt?

For new asphalt driveways in Seaview, you’ll generally be able to drive on it after it has cooled and set enough to prevent rutting—often the same day or within 24 hours depending on weather and the mix. Wet, cool, or rainy shoulder-season conditions can extend curing time, especially because Lower Mainland–Southwest projects often need weather windows for proper placement and compaction. The contractor should provide a specific “wait time” plan for your driveway, including traffic restrictions and how long vehicles should be kept off to avoid imprinting. If you’re comparing contractors, ask whether they include proper compaction practices and how they schedule around rain so you can understand the realistic timeline.

What causes potholes and how are they repaired?

Potholes usually start as water intrusion. In our coastal freeze–thaw pattern, water can enter small surface cracks, soften the base, and then expand during freezing, which breaks the asphalt or concrete further. Common Seaview triggers include poor edge drainage, thin surfacing over weak spots, and delayed repair of small cracks. Repairs generally fall into two categories: (1) localized patching where the contractor cuts out the damaged area to sound material, addresses any base soft spots, and compacts properly before placing asphalt; or (2) broader remediation if there are widespread failures suggesting base drainage issues. If your driveway is still within the expected lifespan but has isolated defects, patching is usually the best value; if the failures are frequent, resurfacing can be more cost-effective—often aligning with asphalt pricing in the $12 – $20 per sq ft range for full-scope work.
