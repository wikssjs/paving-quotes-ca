Driveway paving options and costs in Kensington

Kensington, Alberta is a small community on the edge of Calgary’s orbit, and that matters for paving quotes because even short driveways can require extra mobilization time for crews and equipment. With a 2021 population of 3,488 people (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census), work tends to cluster in the late-spring to early-fall window, so scheduling pressure can influence price. Housing mix also drives demand: most private driveway paving in Kensington is tied to single-detached homes, where owners want durable, low-maintenance surfaces that can handle long winters and frequent freeze–thaw. In the Calgary economic region, frost commonly penetrates to about 1.2 m, and that depth is one reason installers keep emphasizing thicker, well-compacted granular bases and positive drainage—without that, you’ll see heaving, cracking, and raveling sooner. The cold continental climate also means hot-mix asphalt placements depend on warmer air and pavement temperatures (typically above 10°C), so asphalt pricing can firm up during peak weeks. If you’re in the more developed portions near the core of Kensington (where vehicle access is straightforward but winters can be harsh), expect contractors to prioritize properly prepared edges and runoff control to protect both the pavement and adjacent landscaping.

What affects the price of paving in Kensington

Even when two homeowners in Kensington describe the same “new driveway,” quotes can differ by 30–50% across Calgary and broader Alberta because the real drivers—base design, drainage corrections, and demolition—aren’t always visible until an installer exposes the existing subgrade. In cold continental climates, frost line depth and freeze–thaw cycles are decisive cost factors. With frost routinely penetrating to about 1.2 m, you typically need a thicker, properly compacted granular base and a well-graded aggregate structure to reduce subgrade movement. That adds material and labour because contractors must excavate deeper, haul and spread more base rock, and compact it in lifts. The compressed paving season also tightens demand; hot-mix asphalt generally requires air temperatures above about 10°C for safe placement and compaction, so spring and early summer scheduling pressure can push asphalt costs toward the upper end of the $12–$20 range. For concrete, mix design matters too—freeze–thaw performance depends on air entrainment and correct curing practices.

In Kensington, two common examples that raise cost are (1) homes with older, poorly draining soils where the contractor needs to reshape the slope and improve runoff paths, and (2) driveways with existing asphalt that fails at the edges, requiring more tear-out than expected. A driveway upgrade can lower cost when the grading is already close (minimal rework) and the existing base holds together, allowing an asphalt overlay instead of full rebuild. To keep numbers grounded, it’s common to see asphalt paving come in around the mid-to-upper $12–$20 band when the base is rebuilt properly, while interlocking pavers often start at the lower end of the $18–$30 band but justify themselves when you anticipate localized repairs rather than replacing the entire surface.

Permits & regulations in Alberta

In Alberta, paving a private driveway within your own property typically does not require a building permit, as long as you’re not altering major drainage infrastructure or creating work that changes how stormwater is handled. However, work in the municipal right-of-way can require approval. That includes new or modified curb cuts, changes to a boulevard area, and any work that affects storm drainage or municipal services. Some municipalities also have lot-grading bylaws, which can restrict how close to property lines a hard surface can be and how runoff must be directed.

For Kensington homeowners, start with utilities: always arrange utility locates through Alberta One-Call before any excavation. Next, confirm lot-grading expectations with the local municipality (especially if your driveway ties into a ditch/boulevard area or you’re changing slopes).

Then, verify your contractor properly—this is where homeowners can protect themselves. Ask for a copy of their liability insurance and confirm the certificate lists your property address or at least the correct legal entity. For licensing, check the Alberta registry for the contractor’s business authorization (and ensure the person doing the work is covered under the company you’re hiring). If applicable to the trade, confirm coverage for workers through WCB/WSIB-equivalent arrangements in Alberta; request proof rather than assuming. In short, utilities locates + municipal approvals (only if right-of-way or drainage changes are involved) + proper insurance/coverage should be handled before excavation begins.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Kensington driveways

For Kensington’s freeze–thaw winters, the “best” driveway material usually comes down to your tolerance for maintenance and your preference for repair style. Asphalt typically has the lowest upfront cost and offers a smooth ride, but it must be sealed periodically (commonly every 3–5 years) to protect against moisture intrusion and winter wear. Interlocking concrete pavers cost more upfront, yet they’re highly repairable—if a section settles or is damaged, you can lift and replace individual stones instead of tearing up the whole surface. Poured concrete lands in the middle on price, but it must be air-entrained and properly finished, because Alberta freeze–thaw cycles can drive cracking and surface deterioration when concrete isn’t designed for cold exposure.

Across Calgary’s frost-influenced conditions, base preparation is non-negotiable for all three. Expect contractors to plan thicker granular bases and careful drainage to reduce water-related expansion under the slab or asphalt. For snow and ice control, pavers usually shrug off routine plowing and salt better because the surface is modular, while asphalt can be more vulnerable at edges and joints if drainage isn’t corrected. Concrete’s durability is excellent when it’s properly engineered and cured, but if water gets trapped at joints or if de-icing chemicals are used aggressively on unsealed or poorly finished concrete, the surface can show wear earlier.

Here’s a practical dollar example: if your driveway is small and you only need a surface treatment, asphalt at roughly $12–$20 per sq ft can undercut pavers by a noticeable margin. But if you’re on a property where repairs are likely (older base, uneven drainage history), the paver system’s “repair one section” advantage can justify the $18–$30 band over time.

How to choose a paving contractor in Kensington

Choosing the right contractor matters in Kensington because base preparation and winter durability come from workmanship details, not marketing. Start by confirming Alberta business authorization: look up the contractor through provincial registry resources (availability varies by trade type), then verify the company name on the quote matches the licensed/registered entity. Next, require a current certificate of liability insurance naming the correct legal entity, and keep an eye on expiry dates—no certificate, no job. For worker protection coverage, request proof of WCB/WCB-equivalent coverage in Alberta where applicable; again, ask for documentation rather than relying on verbal confirmation.

Get 2–3 written, itemized quotes—not one lump-sum number. You want a labour + materials breakdown that shows excavation depth, base type and depth, compaction steps, asphalt mix type, and concrete finish/jointing approach. Read the scope carefully: is permit or right-of-way approval responsibility included where needed? Is disposal/haul-away included for tear-out? Are edge restraints and proper drainage/grading adjustments included? If they’re silent, treat it as excluded.

For warranty, ask for (1) workmanship warranty length, (2) whether product/manufacturer warranty applies to the specific materials they install, and (3) whether it’s transferable if you sell the home. Payment should be structured so you don’t fund the project upfront—typically no more than 10–15% at the start, with holdback until completion and punch-list items are addressed. Finally, insist on a written timeline with a start date window and completion estimate, especially because the paving season is compressed in this region.

Frequently asked questions — paving in Kensington

What is a chip seal driveway and is it suited to Kensington?

Chip seal (tar-and-chip) is a surface treatment where a binder (asphalt emulsion) is sprayed, then aggregate chips are spread and rolled in. It can be a lower-cost way to improve a rough gravel surface, but in Kensington (and the wider Calgary economic region) it depends heavily on base preparation and drainage. If water can sit under the surface during Alberta freeze–thaw cycles, chips can loosen and the surface can ravel sooner than homeowners expect. For many driveways, chip seal is best as a short- to medium-term solution for low-traffic areas, with realistic expectations on lifespan. Cost-wise, it’s often below typical asphalt driveway paving, sometimes compared against the $12–$20 per sq ft asphalt range—however, you may end up paying for repeat re-surfacing if the base or drainage isn’t engineered properly.

How much does driveway crack repair cost in Kensington?

Crack repair in Kensington usually comes down to how wide the crack is and whether it’s structural (related to base movement) or just surface shrinkage. For narrow, stable cracks, contractors commonly use cleaning and crack sealing, while wider or moving cracks may require localized saw-cut removal and patching. As a planning number, homeowners often budget within the general sealing & repair band of $400–$1,500, with the higher end for more extensive preparation, additional labour, or several crack locations. The big variable is whether repairs can be “spot fixes” or whether the contractor recommends addressing drainage or base issues first—especially important in freeze–thaw areas where water expands. A good quote will describe crack type, prep steps, and whether the repair is preventative (sealant) or corrective (patch after removing failed material).

What base thickness do I need for pavers in Kensington?

For pavers in Kensington, base thickness is driven by frost and your site conditions. With frost penetrating deeply in the Calgary region, you generally need a properly compacted granular base thick enough to prevent subgrade movement during repeated freezing and thawing. While exact thickness depends on your soil and drainage, the practical approach most crews follow is a deeper granular build-up than you’d see in milder climates, often involving an excavate-and-rebuild strategy rather than a thin “topping” layer. Your quote should specify the total base depth and the material gradation (and confirm compaction methods). If the existing subgrade is soft or repeatedly wet, base depth may need to be increased, and that’s where costs rise—because more excavation and hauling are required. If you’re quoted only an installation price without a granular build-up plan, it’s usually not a complete paver system for Alberta winters.

How do I get the most accurate paving quote in Kensington?

To get an accurate quote in Kensington, start with an on-site visit so the contractor can verify existing base conditions, drainage, and the required excavation depth. Ask for an itemized proposal showing: tear-out scope (what’s removed and disposed), base thickness, aggregate type, compaction steps, and how edges will be restrained. For asphalt, ask what mix design is being used for freeze–thaw performance and whether sealing will be recommended after curing. For concrete, ask about air entrainment, finishing method (broom finish vs exposed aggregate), and jointing plan—these details affect cracking risk in Alberta’s winter. Also request clarity on exclusions: permit/right-of-way approval responsibility (if needed), disposal and hauling, and any landscaping restoration. Finally, compare like-for-like: if one quote includes proper base rebuild and drainage corrections and another doesn’t, that gap explains much of the price variation you see across Alberta and Calgary-market projects.

How much does it cost to pave a driveway in Kensington?

The cost to pave a driveway in Kensington depends mainly on square footage, base depth, and whether drainage or tear-out is required. For asphalt driveway paving, many residential projects land in the $12–$20 per sq ft range when proper base work is included. Interlocking pavers are typically higher, often within the $18–$30 per sq ft band due to edge restraints, bedding sand, and the heavier installation process. Poured concrete commonly falls into the $15–$25 per sq ft range, with exposed aggregate tending toward the upper side. Smaller driveways can cost more per square foot because mobilization costs are spread over less area. If you share your driveway dimensions and whether there’s existing asphalt or gravel, a contractor should estimate total tonnage/materials and provide a clear breakdown—not just a single number.

Asphalt vs interlocking pavers vs concrete — which is best in Kensington?

In Kensington’s freeze–thaw climate, all three can perform well when the base and drainage are engineered correctly—but “best” depends on how you want to maintain the driveway and how you prefer repairs. Asphalt is usually the best fit for homeowners who want the lowest upfront cost and can commit to routine sealing (often every 3–5 years). Interlocking concrete pavers are often the best choice for curb appeal and for homeowners who want repair flexibility—individual stones can be replaced rather than tearing out the entire surface if a localized issue occurs. Poured concrete is a strong option for a clean, low-maintenance look, but it needs air entrainment and proper finishing/jointing to limit cracking during Alberta winter cycles. As a rough guide, if you’re comparing budgets: asphalt at about $12–$20 per sq ft can be a better entry point, while pavers at $18–$30 per sq ft are frequently worth it when durability and repairability are the priority.
