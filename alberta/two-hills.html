Driveway paving options and costs in Two Hills

Driveway paving in Two Hills typically comes down to three common choices: asphalt, interlocking pavers, or concrete. Two Hills has a high share of detached homes—single-detached dwellings make up 88.8% of the housing stock—so most private driveways are tied to individual homeowners, not multi-unit lots. With 295 homeowner households (66.3% of households own), demand for durable, low-maintenance surfaces is steady, especially on older streets where homes built before 1981 make up 77.5% of the market (Statistics Canada, 2021 Census). In practical terms, that older housing stock often means more driveway retrofits: regrading, correcting drainage, and tying into existing slopes rather than starting from a clean slate.

In the Camrose–Drumheller region, costs are shaped by winter conditions that are harsher than milder Canadian markets. Frost depth in Alberta is commonly in the 6 to 10+ foot range depending on soil and location, and the freeze-thaw cycling can work on weak base and trapped water. Contractors in Two Hills also operate in a shorter paving season because hot-mix asphalt needs warm air temperatures (above about 10°C) for proper paving and curing. When demand tightens in spring, scheduling pressure can affect labour availability and the final quote.

What affects the price of paving in Two Hills

In Two Hills, it’s common to see quotes for what looks like the “same driveway” vary by 30–50% once you zoom in on base thickness, drainage fixes, and excavation conditions. Across the Camrose–Drumheller region and Alberta more broadly, contractors price risk: heavier frost protection requirements and the need to rebuild weak subgrade push up costs when water can’t be controlled. If one contractor designs around the frost protection reality and another relies on an existing base that’s marginal, you’ll feel that difference later—usually after the first tough winter.

Frost line depth and freeze-thaw cycles are decisive cost factors in Alberta. When the subgrade repeatedly freezes and thaws, water expansion and contraction can lift weak sections and open the path for cracks. Colder conditions generally require a thicker, well-compacted granular base—think 6–12 inches in many Alberta driveway builds depending on soil and site conditions—so you don’t get frost heave and settlement. That base thickness directly affects hauling, material volume, compaction time, and sometimes the excavation depth. The paving season is also shorter than in milder markets. Hot-mix asphalt typically needs air temperatures above about 10°C for proper placement and curing, so spring scheduling pressure can raise labour costs during peak demand.

Concrete pricing can move too based on winter detailing. In Two Hills, a broom-finished slab without proper joint planning is more likely to show cracking patterns sooner because freeze-thaw stresses the slab. Exposed aggregate can add cost for finish and curing attention. Meanwhile, if you’re overlaying asphalt onto a gravel base that still holds water, you may pay less upfront but face earlier patching—often pushing the effective lifetime cost closer to the “new installation” band of about $6,000–$15,000.

Bottom line: in a home built around the 1970s or earlier (a large share of Two Hills’ stock), you frequently inherit older drainage paths and tougher excavation. If the contractor has to correct slope and install drainage, that’s where your budget can jump—sometimes moving from a mid-range asphalt plan toward a paver-ready drainage-and-base system in the $9,000–$22,000 band.

Permits & regulations in Alberta

In Alberta, paving a private driveway on your own property typically does not require a building permit. If the work is confined to your lot—such as removing and replacing asphalt, pouring a new slab on approved base, or installing interlocking pavers within your property line—permits are often not triggered. However, work in the municipal right-of-way usually does require municipal approval, even if it’s “just a driveway update.” That includes new or expanded curb cuts, changes to boulevard grading, and any altered storm drainage that affects ditch flow or culverts.

Some municipalities also have lot-grading or surface-drainage expectations that restrict how close a hard surface can be to property lines and require that water be directed appropriately (rather than allowed to run toward neighbouring properties or sit under the slab). In practice, the drainage design is what prevents freeze-thaw failures, so it’s worth aligning your contractor’s plan with local lot-drainage expectations.

Step one for any Two Hills homeowner is utility locates. Call Alberta One-Call before excavation so your contractor can mark buried services. Step two is to confirm the contractor’s credentials: ask for their Alberta licence number (where applicable to their work scope), a current certificate of liability insurance, and evidence of WSIB/WCB coverage. You can verify the insurance certificate dates and coverage limits directly from the contractor’s paperwork, and you can often cross-check licensing details through the appropriate provincial registry listings. A contractor should also be able to provide clearance or compliance letters if your municipality asks for documentation related to right-of-way work.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Two Hills driveways

For Two Hills driveways, asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete each have a “best at” profile when you factor in freeze-thaw, snow-plough patterns, and winter maintenance habits. Asphalt is usually the lowest upfront cost and gives a smooth ride, but it depends on proper base prep and it needs sealing to protect against oxidation and surface wear. In Alberta winters, sealing timing matters; many contractors recommend sealing within the first 6–12 months after installation, then every 3–5 years based on wear.

Interlocking concrete pavers often cost more upfront, but they’re highly repairable. When freeze-thaw movement is present, pavers can be lifted, the base can be corrected, and the affected area rebuilt without tearing out the entire driveway. That can be a big advantage when you’re dealing with older foundations or drainage corrections common in a neighbourhood setting like Main Street, where tie-ins and grades can be tight.

Poured concrete (including broom-finished slabs) typically falls in the middle to upper range. It can last a long time when it’s air-entrained and properly jointed, but it is more susceptible to cracking if the slab design doesn’t account for frost stresses and moisture movement. Exposed aggregate increases traction for snowy conditions, but it still relies on correct base and drainage.

In terms of base depth, all three need frost-protection-grade compacted granular layers in Alberta—often 6–12 inches depending on soil and site conditions. When drainage is done right, asphalt can perform very well; when drainage is tricky, pavers may offer the safest long-term repair path. For a realistic dollar example: if you’re budgeting around the asphalt band of $6,000–$15,000, spending toward the paver level of $9,000–$22,000 can be justified when you expect future base access, want curb appeal, or need a system that can be maintained section-by-section after a tough winter.

How to choose a paving contractor in Two Hills

Choosing the right paving contractor in Two Hills is less about flashy ads and more about proof. Start by verifying that the contractor is properly licensed for their work scope in Alberta, then check liability insurance and WSIB/WCB coverage. Ask for a current certificate of insurance (with coverage dates and job-site/operations listed) and confirm their workers are covered under the correct provincial insurance program. If you can’t get documents up front, that’s a practical red flag—because uncured coverage issues can become your problem if someone is hurt on your property.

Next, request 2–3 itemised written quotes. You want a labour-and-materials breakdown (excavation, haul-away, granular base, compaction, paving/pavers/concrete, edge restraints, and finishing). Avoid “lump sum only” quotes because differences hide in the scope: disposal included or not, permit work included or not, and whether drainage corrections are included are the most common sources of mid-project surprises. Clarify start date, weather-related contingencies, and the estimated completion window in writing—important in an Alberta season where curing and frost protection depend on timing.

Warranty matters in a cold climate. Ask for the workmanship warranty length and what it covers (and for how long). Also ask whether the product/manufacturer warranty applies to the specific materials used and whether it’s transferable to a homeowner. For payment schedule, never pay more than 10–15% upfront; hold back a portion until you’ve completed final grading, clean-up, and punch-list items. With older lots and variable drainage, a good contractor earns their final payment by closing out the details, not by rushing to mobilize equipment again.

  • Request Alberta insurance and confirm coverage dates before any excavation begins.
  • Verify WSIB/WCB coverage for the crew performing the work.
  • Ask for an itemised quote: excavation, base thickness, compaction, drainage, surface course, and finishing.
  • Confirm disposal/haul-away is included for removed asphalt or concrete.
  • Ensure drainage corrections are described (slope, culverts/ditch tie-in if applicable).
  • Get edge restraint details in writing for pavers and the thickness plan for asphalt.
  • Ask what happens if weather delays curing or asphalt placement (written contingency plan).
  • Review warranty terms: workmanship, exclusions, and response timeline for repairs.
  • Confirm whether the contractor will seal asphalt within the recommended window after installation.
  • Ask for a material spec: freeze-thaw rated asphalt mix or air-entrainment for concrete.
  • Use a payment schedule with no more than 10–15% upfront and a holdback to completion.
  • Get a start date, milestones, and a completion estimate in the contract.

Red flags to watch for in Two Hills: contractors who won’t provide insurance/coverage paperwork; quotes that skip base thickness and drainage (or treat them as “optional later”); no written scope for disposal and cleanup; vague warranties with no workmanship terms; and pressure to pay large deposits upfront before base prep and forms/compaction are verified.

How do I get the most accurate paving quote in Two Hills?

A good first step is a site visit where they measure existing driveway thickness, check how water moves after thaw, and confirm whether any right-of-way area is involved. In this part of Alberta, frost-protection design hinges on base depth and subgrade moisture control, so a quote that ignores base thickness is usually underestimating the real cost. Request an itemised labour/materials breakdown rather than a lump sum, and ask whether asphalt mix design meets freeze-thaw performance needs. If you want a budget check, you can compare results against typical asphalt installation pricing of $6,000–$15,000 for many driveway scopes, but only after confirming drainage and base work are included.

How much does it cost to pave a driveway in Two Hills?

Driveway paving costs in Two Hills vary most with square footage, excavation depth, and how much drainage correction is needed. For asphalt, many homeowners land in the range of $6,000–$15,000 when the contractor builds a proper base and does a full new installation. If your driveway needs extensive tear-out and grading—or you have tight access that slows equipment—expect the higher end of the band. If you choose interlocking concrete pavers, the typical budget often shifts upward to $9,000–$22,000 because the system includes base, bedding sand, restraints, and more detailed laying. Concrete tends to fall between: poured concrete or exposed aggregate commonly fits around $7,500–$18,000 depending on finish and thickness. In cold Alberta conditions, paying for the right base and drainage usually costs more upfront but helps avoid early settlement, heaving, and recurring patching.

Asphalt vs interlocking pavers vs concrete — which is best in Two Hills?

There isn’t one “best” material for every Two Hills driveway, but there’s a best match. Asphalt is often best when you want the lowest upfront cost and a smooth, uniform surface, as long as the contractor builds a deeper compacted granular base for freeze-thaw conditions and seals on schedule. Interlocking concrete pavers are often best when you want curb appeal and easy repair—if a section shifts after a hard winter, the contractor can lift and reset that area without tearing out the entire driveway. Poured concrete can be a strong choice too, especially when the mix is air-entrained and the slab is properly jointed for Alberta freeze-thaw stresses. For winter maintenance, snow plough traffic and sanding habits make base quality and drainage more important than the surface layer alone. If your quote difference is about the jump from asphalt pricing (often $6,000–$15,000) to pavers (often $9,000–$22,000), that extra cost is most justified when drainage is tricky or when you value repairability over replacing the whole driveway later.

How long does a paved driveway last in Two Hills?

Typical lifespan in Two Hills depends heavily on base design, drainage, and how soon sealing or maintenance happens. Asphalt with a proper granular base commonly lasts about 15–25 years, but only when it’s sealed within the first 6–12 months and then every 3–5 years (based on wear). Interlocking pavers often last 25–40 years because repairs can be localized—so the driveway can recover after minor freeze-thaw movement without full replacement. Poured concrete generally lasts 20–30 years when it’s air-entrained and properly jointed for Alberta conditions. Exposed aggregate can last similar to other concrete options when joints and curing are handled correctly. If your driveway has trapped moisture or weak subgrade from an older installation, no surface is immune: the freeze-thaw cycle can accelerate edge lift, settlement, and cracking regardless of whether you choose asphalt, pavers, or concrete.

When is the best time of year to pave in Two Hills?

In Two Hills, the best time is typically late spring through early fall, when the weather supports proper placement and curing. Asphalt needs warm air temperatures—commonly above about 10°C—so crews often start once consistent daytime conditions return. The tighter the schedule in colder regions like Camrose–Drumheller, the more important it is to book early, because delays can compress the remaining paving window. Concrete pours also need weather planning: avoid conditions that risk freezing before initial set and ensure proper curing so the slab develops strength. For any material, base prep and drainage correction should not be rushed—grading and compaction need workable conditions so the structure doesn’t trap water. If your contractor suggests paving during marginal temperatures, ask how they’ll manage curing and freeze protection to reduce winter failures in Alberta.

How deep should the base be for Two Hills's frost?

Base depth in Two Hills should be designed for frost protection and moisture control rather than using a one-size number. Alberta’s freeze-thaw cycling can punish shallow or poorly compacted base, especially where water collects or where the existing subgrade is weak. In practical driveway builds in this region, many contractors plan for a deeper compacted granular base—often in the 6–12 inch range depending on soil conditions, driveway thickness requirements, and whether drainage is being corrected. The goal is to keep water from sitting in the structure so it can’t expand and cause frost heave and cracking. A good quote will explain how they determined base depth (soil assessment, drainage observations, and the proposed thickness of granular layers) rather than just assuming. Because frost depth can vary with site conditions, the “right” base depth for your driveway is determined during the site assessment, not by a generic internet guideline.
