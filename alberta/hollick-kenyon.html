Driveway paving options and costs in Hollick-Kenyon

Calgary’s housing stock is heavily dominated by single-detached neighbourhood development, and that matters: most private driveways here are attached to detached homes, so driveway paving is a recurring, high-impact upgrade rather than a one-off commercial project.

What affects the price of paving in Hollick-Kenyon

Two homeowners in the same part of Hollick-Kenyon can receive quotes that differ by 30–50% for the “same” driveway because the real scope is rarely identical. The big swing factors are subgrade condition, how much base must be rebuilt to handle frost heave, how difficult it is to excavate and haul away material, and whether drainage and edges need upgrading before any asphalt or concrete goes down. In Calgary, the compressed season also tightens scheduling; if your date lands in peak late-spring/early-summer weeks, labour and equipment demand rises and contractors price that risk into smaller jobs.

Frost line depth and freeze–thaw cycles are decisive in this region. With frost reaching roughly 1.2 m (4 ft), deeper granular bases and well-graded aggregates are usually required to prevent subgrade movement and surface failure. Contractors often plan a thicker compacted base than you’d see in milder coastal areas, and the difference directly impacts material cost, hauling, and time for compaction passes. Also, asphalt mix design must be freeze–thaw suited—using the right gradation and binder helps asphalt resist cracking during repeated winter cycles.

Concrete pricing can change quickly in Hollick-Kenyon. For example, a simple broom-finish driveway may cost near the mid-range band, but adding air-entrainment, increasing reinforcement, or adding proper joints and subdrainage can push the project toward the upper end. On the other hand, if your existing grade and drainage are already correct and you’re not doing major tear-out, concrete can land closer to the $15–$25 band. Asphalt may look inexpensive at first, but if the base is failing and needs correction, you may spend closer to the $12–$20 neighbourhood for a proper rebuild rather than a thin patch.

Permits & regulations in Alberta

For most homeowners in Hollick-Kenyon, paving a private driveway within your own property generally does not require a building permit. In practical terms, if you’re repaving an existing driveway footprint, replacing asphalt or concrete on your land, and not changing how water leaves your property, you typically move forward without municipal permits. The exceptions are usually tied to work that affects the municipal right-of-way—especially where your driveway meets the road.

Work that does typically require municipal approval includes changing or creating a new curb cut, modifying the boulevard, altering storm drainage connections, or changing how runoff is handled where it crosses or meets municipal infrastructure. Many municipalities also have lot-grading requirements (even when a permit isn’t triggered) that restrict how close hard surfaces and grading profiles can be to property lines and how water can be directed.

Step-by-step, here’s how to verify a contractor in Alberta before they excavate: first, confirm they are properly licensed for the work they’re advertising and request their liability insurance certificate. Second, ask for proof of coverage for workers—WSIB coverage where applicable (or the equivalent WCB clearance/coverage documentation). Third, request a clearance letter or proof-of-coverage number so your file isn’t left holding the bag if something happens on site. Finally, always call for utility locates before excavation through Alberta One-Call, and make sure your contractor’s locate tickets match your address and planned digging depth.

Asphalt vs pavers vs concrete for Hollick-Kenyon driveways

For Hollick-Kenyon driveways, asphalt, interlocking pavers, and concrete each fit a different homeowner priority, and our freeze–thaw climate makes base preparation and drainage more important than marketing claims. Asphalt is usually the lowest upfront cost and works well when installed over a properly compacted granular base, but it does need sealing every 3–5 years to protect the surface from moisture intrusion during winter. Interlocking concrete pavers typically cost more at the start, yet they shine in Calgary’s weather because individual stones can be lifted, corrected, and re-set if settlement or drainage issues show up. Poured concrete lands in the mid-range, but it can crack under repeated freeze–thaw if it’s not properly air-entrained and detailed with the right joints and subbase.

Because frost penetrates deeply (about 1.2 m / 4 ft), the “quiet” part of driveway performance is the base and drainage: correct slope away from the house, positive runoff management, and edge containment prevent water from freezing under the surface. Salt and sand from winter maintenance also stress surfaces—so the best-performing installs are the ones designed for it, not just the newest-looking ones.

A concrete example: if an asphalt driveway in your area is quoted around the $12–$20 per sq ft band, you might pay roughly $6,000–$10,500 for a typical install depending on area and tear-out needs. Pavers at $18–$30 per sq ft often land $8,500–$16,000, which is justified when you want long-term curb appeal and “repair locally” flexibility rather than resurfacing later. If you don’t expect to stay in the home long, asphalt can be the smart value play—if the base is truly rebuilt. If you’re staying long-term and care about appearance, pavers usually win lifecycle cost and repair convenience.

How to choose a paving contractor in Hollick-Kenyon

When you’re hiring in Alberta—especially in Hollick-Kenyon where frost depth and freeze–thaw drive base requirements—the best quotes come from contractors who explain the build-up in plain language and show you documentation. Start by verifying Alberta licensing or trade eligibility for the scope they’re offering, then ask for a current certificate of liability insurance (make sure your name/address can be referenced). For worker protection, request proof of WSIB or WCB coverage (as applicable) and a clearance letter or coverage confirmation number so you can document it for your records.

Next, get 2–3 itemised written quotes, not lump sums. You want labour and materials broken out: excavation, hauling/disposal, base material, compaction passes, geotextile (if specified), asphalt/concrete placement, forms/edge restraints, and any sealcoat. Read the exclusions line-by-line: is permit/municipal approval included if needed, is turf restoration included, and is disposal included or charged separately? A solid workmanship warranty should be in writing—state the term for labour and what it covers. Also note if the product/manufacturer warranty applies to you directly and whether it’s transferable if you sell your home.

For payment scheduling, don’t pay more than 10–15% upfront. Hold back a portion until you receive the final clean-up and sign-off, and insist on a start date and completion estimate in writing (the paving season is short, so dates matter).

Frequently asked questions — paving in Hollick-Kenyon

Asphalt vs interlocking pavers vs concrete — which is best in Hollick-Kenyon?

In Hollick-Kenyon and across Calgary’s cold freeze–thaw winters, the “best” choice is usually the one built on the right granular base with positive drainage. Asphalt is typically the best value upfront (often in the $12–$20 per sq ft band when the base is done properly) and performs well if you plan sealcoating every 3–5 years. Interlocking pavers cost more (commonly $18–$30 per sq ft), but they’re a strong long-term pick if you want curb appeal and the ability to repair sections without tearing out the whole driveway. Poured concrete can be a good mid-range option ($15–$25 per sq ft), but it must be air-entrained and properly jointed to manage winter movement. If your subgrade is questionable, ask each contractor to explain how they’ll correct grading and water flow before choosing a surface.

How long does a paved driveway last in Hollick-Kenyon?

Driveway lifespan in Alberta depends heavily on base construction and drainage, not just the top layer. Asphalt driveways commonly last about 15–25 years when installed over a correctly compacted granular base and sealed regularly; without sealing, surface oxidation and moisture intrusion can shorten performance. Interlocking pavers often reach 25–35 years because individual stones can be replaced if localized settlement occurs. Poured concrete typically lasts 20–30 years when properly air-entrained, jointed, and protected from water ponding. Exposed aggregate concrete can also run 25–35 years, but patching requires careful finish matching. If your driveway currently shows pumping gravel, edge deterioration, or recurring water pooling, address those issues first—otherwise any surface will fail prematurely, regardless of material.

When is the best time of year to pave in Hollick-Kenyon?

The best window is late spring through early fall, when temperatures and ground conditions allow for compaction and curing. Asphalt generally needs suitable warm air temperatures to place and compact properly—cold placement can lead to poor density and earlier cracking. Even when crews are available, Hollick-Kenyon projects need time for proper base compaction and for concrete to cure safely under Alberta temperature swings. If you’re aiming to minimize disruption and scheduling risk, book earlier in the season rather than waiting for the warmest weeks only—demand spikes as crews complete multiple small driveways and resurfacing jobs. A good contractor will also include weather contingencies in writing (rain delays, freeze risk overnight, and curing/curing protection).

How deep should the base be for Hollick-Kenyon's frost?

Because frost penetration in this region can reach about 1.2 m (4 ft), the driveway’s performance depends more on the engineered base and drainage than on a single “magic number.” In practice, contractors design a deeper, well-compacted granular base for freeze–thaw resistance and water control, and they adjust thickness based on soil condition, whether the driveway is new or an overlay, and how well your grading drains. In Hollick-Kenyon, it’s common to see more substantial base rebuilds than you’d expect in milder climates, particularly if there’s clay subgrade, weak soils, or signs of pumping. The right approach is to ask your contractor to specify the intended base build-up (thickness and materials) and explain how they’re preventing water from staying under the pavement.

How often should I seal my asphalt driveway?

Plan to seal after the asphalt has cured—often after the first season—then follow a cycle of about every 3–5 years in Calgary-area winters. The exact timing depends on sun exposure, traffic load, and how much sand and de-icing residue your driveway takes each season. If you’re seeing faded black surface, fine surface raveling, or moisture darkening after melting periods, it’s usually time to schedule sealing. Sealing doesn’t replace correct base drainage, but it significantly slows surface oxidation and helps reduce moisture intrusion that can worsen cracking. If your contractor quoted asphalt in the $12–$20 per sq ft range, ask them whether their estimate includes sealcoat timing and who supplies and applies it—many lower-cost quotes cover paving only, leaving sealing as a future add-on (often in the $400–$1,500 band depending on size).

Why does my driveway crack and heave in winter?

Cracking and heaving are usually symptoms of water and movement, not just “normal wear.” In Hollick-Kenyon, freeze–thaw cycles can expand water in subgrade and cause heaving when the base is too thin, poorly compacted, or not well drained. If water ponds on the driveway or edges aren’t contained, freeze expansion repeats every winter and can push asphalt up, leading to cracks around wheel paths and near edges. Common triggers include inadequate slope away from the garage/house, missing or failing edge restraint (pavers spread; asphalt edges break down), and overlays placed over material that wasn’t stabilized or corrected. De-icing sand and salts can also accelerate surface breakdown if the top layer isn’t protected with sealcoat. A contractor should assess grade, drainage, and base condition before you repave—otherwise the same failure pattern returns.
